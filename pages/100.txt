<p><b>CiteSeerX</b> (often stylized as <b>CiteSeer<sup>X</sup></b>; formerly called <b>CiteSeer</b>) is a public <a href="/wiki/Search_engine_(computing)" title="Search engine (computing)">search engine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Digital_library" title="Digital library">digital library</a> for scientific and academic papers, primarily in the fields of <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Information_science" title="Information science">information science</a>.
</p><p>CiteSeer's goal is to improve the dissemination and access of academic and scientific literature. As a non-profit service that can be freely used by anyone, it has been considered part of the <a href="/wiki/Open_access_(publishing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Open access (publishing)">open access</a> movement that is attempting to change <a href="/wiki/Academic_publishing" title="Academic publishing">academic and scientific publishing</a> to allow greater access to scientific literature. CiteSeer freely provided <a href="/wiki/Open_Archives_Initiative" title="Open Archives Initiative">Open Archives Initiative</a> <a href="/wiki/Metadata" title="Metadata">metadata</a> of all indexed documents and links indexed documents when possible to other sources of metadata such as <a href="/wiki/DBLP" title="DBLP">DBLP</a> and the <a href="/wiki/ACM_Portal" class="mw-redirect" title="ACM Portal">ACM Portal</a>. To promote <a href="/wiki/Open_data" title="Open data">open data</a>, <b>CiteSeerX</b> shares its data for non-commercial purposes under a <a href="/wiki/Creative_Commons_license" title="Creative Commons license">Creative Commons license</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-CC-license&#95;1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CC-license-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>CiteSeer is considered a predecessor of academic search tools such as <a href="/wiki/Google_Scholar" title="Google Scholar">Google Scholar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Academic_Search" title="Microsoft Academic Search">Microsoft Academic Search</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> CiteSeer-like engines and archives usually only harvest documents from publicly available websites and do not crawl publisher websites. For this reason, authors whose documents are freely available are more likely to be represented in the index.
</p><p>CiteSeer changed its name to ResearchIndex at one point and then changed it back.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CiteSeerX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="CiteSeer_and_CiteSeer.IST">CiteSeer and CiteSeer.IST</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CiteSeerX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: CiteSeer and CiteSeer.IST"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>CiteSeer was created by researchers <a href="/wiki/Lee_Giles" title="Lee Giles">Lee Giles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Bollacker" title="Kurt Bollacker">Kurt Bollacker</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steve_Lawrence_(computer_scientist)" title="Steve Lawrence (computer scientist)">Steve Lawrence</a> in 1997 while they were at the <a href="/wiki/NEC_Research_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="NEC Research Institute">NEC Research Institute</a> (now <a href="/wiki/NEC_Laboratories_America" title="NEC Laboratories America">NEC Labs</a>), <a href="/wiki/Princeton,_New_Jersey" title="Princeton, New Jersey">Princeton, New Jersey</a>, US. CiteSeer's goal was to actively crawl and harvest academic and scientific documents on the web and use autonomous <a href="/wiki/Citation_index" title="Citation index">citation indexing</a> to permit querying by citation or by document, ranking them by <a href="/wiki/Citation_impact" title="Citation impact">citation impact</a>. At one point, it was called ResearchIndex.
</p><p>CiteSeer became public in 1998 and had many new features unavailable in academic search engines at that time. These included:
</p>
<ul><li>Autonomous Citation Indexing automatically created a citation index that can be used for literature search and evaluation.</li>
<li>Citation statistics and related documents were computed for all articles cited in the database, not just the indexed articles.</li>
<li>Reference linking, allowing browsing of the database using citation links.</li>
<li>Citation context showed the context of citations to a given paper, allowing a researcher to quickly and easily see what other researchers have to say about an article of interest.</li>
<li>Related documents were shown using citation and word based measures, and an active and continuously updated bibliography is shown for each document.</li></ul>
<p>CiteSeer was granted a United States <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a> #&#160;6289342, titled "<i>Autonomous citation indexing and literature browsing using citation context</i><span style="padding-left:.15em;">"</span>, on September 11, 2001. The patent was filed on May 20, 1998, and has priority to January 5, 1998. A continuation patent (US Patent #&#160;6738780) was filed on May 16, 2001, and granted on May 18, 2004.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2023)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>After NEC, in 2004 it was hosted as CiteSeer.IST on the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a> at the College of Information Sciences and Technology, The <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_State_University" title="Pennsylvania State University">Pennsylvania State University</a>, and had over 700,000 documents. For enhanced access, performance and research, similar versions of CiteSeer were supported at universities such as the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Z%C3%BCrich" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Zürich">University of Zürich</a> and the <a href="/wiki/National_University_of_Singapore" title="National University of Singapore">National University of Singapore</a>. However, these versions of CiteSeer proved difficult to maintain and are no longer available. Because CiteSeer only indexes freely available papers on the web and does not have access to publisher metadata, it returns fewer citation counts than sites, such as <a href="/wiki/Google_Scholar" title="Google Scholar">Google Scholar</a>, that have publisher metadata.
</p><p>CiteSeer had not been comprehensively updated since 2005 due to limitations in its architecture design. It had a representative sampling of research documents in computer and information science but was limited in coverage because it was limited to papers that are publicly available, usually at an author's homepage, or those submitted by an author. To overcome some of these limitations, a modular and open source architecture for CiteSeer was designed&#160;&#8211;&#32; CiteSeerX.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="CiteSeerX">CiteSeerX</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CiteSeerX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: CiteSeerX"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>CiteSeerX replaced CiteSeer and all queries to CiteSeer were redirected. CiteSeerX<sup id="cite&#95;ref-about-page&#95;4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about-page-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> is a public <a href="/wiki/Search_engine" title="Search engine">search engine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Digital_library" title="Digital library">digital library</a> and <a href="/wiki/Disciplinary_repository" title="Disciplinary repository">repository</a> for scientific and academic papers, primarily with a focus on <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Information_science" title="Information science">information science</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-about-page&#95;4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about-page-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However, recently CiteSeerX has been expanding into other scholarly domains such as economics, physics and others. Released in 2008, it was loosely based on the previous CiteSeer search engine and digital library and is built with a new <a href="/wiki/Open-source_software" title="Open-source software">open source</a> infrastructure, SeerSuite, and new algorithms and their implementations. It was developed by researchers Isaac Councill and C. <a href="/wiki/Lee_Giles" title="Lee Giles">Lee Giles</a> at <a href="/wiki/Penn_State_College_of_Information_Sciences_and_Technology" class="mw-redirect" title="Penn State College of Information Sciences and Technology">the College of Information Sciences and Technology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_State_University" title="Pennsylvania State University">Pennsylvania State University</a>. It continues to support the goals outlined by CiteSeer to actively crawl and harvest academic and scientific documents on the public web and to use a citation inquiry by citations and ranking of documents by the impact of citations. Currently, Lee Giles, Prasenjit Mitra, Susan Gauch, Min-Yen Kan, Pradeep Teregowda, Juan Pablo Fernández Ramírez, Pucktada Treeratpituk, Jian Wu, Douglas Jordan, Steve Carman, Jack Carroll, Jim Jansen, and Shuyi Zheng are or have been actively involved in its development. Recently, a table search feature was introduced.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It has been funded by the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a>, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Research" title="Microsoft Research">Microsoft Research</a>.
</p><p>CiteSeerX continues to be rated as one of the world's top repositories, and was rated number 1 in July 2010.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It currently has over 6 million documents with nearly 6 million unique authors and 120 million citations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Relative_time_references" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="Clarify the applicable timeframe so that it is unambiguous when read at a later date. (June 2023)">timeframe?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>CiteSeerX also shares its software, data, databases and metadata with other researchers, currently by <a href="/wiki/Amazon_S3" title="Amazon S3">Amazon S3</a> and by <a href="/wiki/Rsync" title="Rsync">rsync</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Its new modular open source architecture and software (available previously on <a href="/wiki/SourceForge" title="SourceForge">SourceForge</a> but now on <a href="/wiki/GitHub" title="GitHub">GitHub</a>) is built on <a href="/wiki/Apache_Solr" title="Apache Solr">Apache Solr</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Apache_Software_Foundation" class="mw-redirect" title="Apache Software Foundation">Apache</a> and open source tools, which allows it to be a testbed for new algorithms in document harvesting, ranking, indexing, and information extraction.
</p><p>CiteSeerX caches some PDF files that it has scanned.  As such, each page includes a <a href="/wiki/DMCA" class="mw-redirect" title="DMCA">DMCA</a> link which can be used to report copyright violations.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Current_features">Current features</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CiteSeerX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Current features"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Automated_information_extraction">Automated information extraction</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CiteSeerX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Automated information extraction"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>CiteSeerX uses automated <a href="/wiki/Information_extraction" title="Information extraction">information extraction</a> tools, usually built on machine learning methods such ParsCit, to extract scholarly document metadata such as title, authors, abstract, citations, etc. As such, there are sometime errors in authors and titles. Other academic search engines have similar errors.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Focused_crawling">Focused crawling</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CiteSeerX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Focused crawling"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>CiteSeerX crawls publicly available scholarly documents primarily from author webpages and other open resources, and does not have access to publisher metadata. As such, citation counts in CiteSeerX are usually less than those in Google Scholar and Microsoft Academic Search who have access to publisher metadata.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Usage">Usage</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CiteSeerX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Usage"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>CiteSeerX has nearly one million users worldwide based on unique IP addresses and has millions of hits daily. Annual downloads of document PDFs were nearly 200 million for 2015.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Data">Data</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CiteSeerX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Data"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>CiteSeerX data is regularly shared under a <a href="/wiki/Creative_Commons_license" title="Creative Commons license">Creative Commons BY-NC-SA license</a> with researchers worldwide and has been and is used in many experiments and competitions.
</p><p>Thanks to its <a href="/wiki/OAI-PMH" class="mw-redirect" title="OAI-PMH">OAI-PMH</a> endpoint,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> CiteSeerX is an <a href="/wiki/Open_archive" class="mw-redirect" title="Open archive">open archive</a> and its content is indexed like an <a href="/wiki/Institutional_repository" title="Institutional repository">institutional repository</a> in <a href="/wiki/Academic_search_engines" class="mw-redirect" title="Academic search engines">academic search engines</a>, for instance <a href="/wiki/BASE_(search_engine)" title="BASE (search engine)">BASE</a> and <a href="/wiki/Unpaywall" class="mw-redirect" title="Unpaywall">Unpaywall</a> consumers.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Other_SeerSuite-based_search_engines">Other SeerSuite-based search engines</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CiteSeerX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other SeerSuite-based search engines"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The CiteSeer model had been extended to cover academic documents in business with <a href="/wiki/SmealSearch" class="mw-redirect" title="SmealSearch">SmealSearch</a> and in e-business with <a href="/w/index.php?title=EBizSearch&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="EBizSearch (page does not exist)">eBizSearch</a>.  However, these were not maintained by their sponsors. An older version of both of these could be once found at <a href="/w/index.php?title=BizSeer.IST&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="BizSeer.IST (page does not exist)">BizSeer.IST</a> but is no longer in service.
</p><p>Other Seer-like search and repository systems have been built for chemistry (<a href="/wiki/ChemXSeer" title="ChemXSeer">ChemXSeer</a>), and for archaeology (ArchSeer). Another had been built for <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886049734">.mw-parser-output .monospaced{font-family:monospace,monospace}</style><span class="monospaced">robots.txt</span> file search, <a href="/wiki/BotSeer" title="BotSeer">BotSeer</a>. All of these are built on the open source tool <a href="/w/index.php?title=SeerSuite&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="SeerSuite (page does not exist)">SeerSuite</a>, which uses the open source indexer <a href="/wiki/Lucene" class="mw-redirect" title="Lucene">Lucene</a>.
</p>
