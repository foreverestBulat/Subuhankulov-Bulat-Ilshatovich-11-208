<p><b>Google Scholar</b> is a freely accessible <a href="/wiki/Web_search_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Web search engine">web search engine</a> that indexes the full text or <a href="/wiki/Metadata" title="Metadata">metadata</a> of <a href="/wiki/Academic_publishing" title="Academic publishing">scholarly literature</a> across an array of publishing formats and disciplines. Released in <a href="/wiki/Beta_release" class="mw-redirect" title="Beta release">beta</a> in November 2004, the Google Scholar index includes <a href="/wiki/Peer-reviewed" class="mw-redirect" title="Peer-reviewed">peer-reviewed</a> online <a href="/wiki/Academic_journal" title="Academic journal">academic journals</a> and books, conference papers, <a href="/wiki/Theses" class="mw-redirect" title="Theses">theses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dissertation" class="mw-redirect" title="Dissertation">dissertations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Preprint" title="Preprint">preprints</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abstract_(summary)" title="Abstract (summary)">abstracts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Technical_report" title="Technical report">technical reports</a>, and other scholarly literature, including <a href="/wiki/Legal_opinion" title="Legal opinion">court opinions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patents</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Google&#95;Scholar&#95;Content&#95;Coverage&#95;1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Google_Scholar_Content_Coverage-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Google Scholar uses a web crawler, or web robot, to identify files for inclusion in the search results.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> For content to be indexed in Google Scholar, it must meet certain specified criteria.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> An earlier statistical estimate published in <a href="/wiki/PLOS_One" title="PLOS One">PLOS One</a> using a <a href="/wiki/Mark_and_recapture" title="Mark and recapture">mark and recapture</a> method estimated approximately 79–90% coverage of all articles published in English with an estimate of 100 million.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-nat&#95;22&#95;5&#95;2014&#95;4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nat_22_5_2014-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This estimate also determined how many online documents were available. Google Scholar has been criticized for not vetting journals and for including <a href="/wiki/Predatory_open_access_publishing" class="mw-redirect" title="Predatory open access publishing">predatory journals</a> in its index.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The University of Michigan Library and other libraries whose collections Google scanned for <a href="/wiki/Google_Books" title="Google Books">Google Books</a> and Google Scholar retained copies of the scans and have used them to create the <a href="/wiki/HathiTrust_Digital_Library" class="mw-redirect" title="HathiTrust Digital Library">HathiTrust Digital Library</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-umlibrary&#95;6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umlibrary-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2></div>
<p>Google Scholar arose out of a discussion between Alex Verstak and <a href="/wiki/Anurag_Acharya" title="Anurag Acharya">Anurag Acharya</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> both of whom were then working on building Google's main web index.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Their goal was to "make the world's problem solvers 10% more efficient"<sup id="cite&#95;ref-backchannel2015&#95;11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-backchannel2015-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> by allowing easier and more accurate access to scientific knowledge. This goal is reflected in the Google Scholar's advertising slogan "<a href="/wiki/Standing_on_the_shoulders_of_giants" title="Standing on the shoulders of giants">Stand on the shoulders of giants</a>", which was taken from an idea attributed to <a href="/wiki/Bernard_of_Chartres" title="Bernard of Chartres">Bernard of Chartres</a>, <a href="https://en.wikiquote.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton" class="extiw" title="q:Isaac Newton">quoted by Isaac Newton</a>, and is a nod to the scholars who have contributed to their fields over the centuries, providing the foundation for new intellectual achievements.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> One of the sources for the texts in Google Scholar is the University of Michigan's print collection.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-umlibrary&#95;6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umlibrary-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Scholars have gained a range of features over time. In 2006, a citation importing feature was implemented supporting <a href="/wiki/Reference_management_software" title="Reference management software">bibliography managers</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/RefWorks" title="RefWorks">RefWorks</a>, <a href="/wiki/RefMan" class="mw-redirect" title="RefMan">RefMan</a>, <a href="/wiki/EndNote" title="EndNote">EndNote</a>, and <a href="/wiki/BibTeX" title="BibTeX">BibTeX</a>. In 2007, Acharya announced that Google Scholar had started a program to digitize and host journal articles in agreement with their publishers, an effort separate from <a href="/wiki/Google_Books" title="Google Books">Google Books</a>, whose scans of older journals do not include the metadata required for identifying specific articles in specific issues.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 2011, <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> removed Scholar from the toolbars on its search pages,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> making it both less easily accessible and less discoverable for users not already aware of its existence. Around this period, sites with similar features such as <a href="/wiki/CiteSeer" class="mw-redirect" title="CiteSeer">CiteSeer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scirus" title="Scirus">Scirus</a>, and Microsoft <a href="/wiki/Windows_Live_Academic" class="mw-redirect" title="Windows Live Academic">Windows Live Academic</a> search were developed. Some of these are now defunct; in 2016, Microsoft launched a new competitor, <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Academic" title="Microsoft Academic">Microsoft Academic</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>A major enhancement was rolled out in 2012, with the possibility for individual scholars to create personal "Scholar Citations profiles".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-profiles2014&#95;16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-profiles2014-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A feature introduced in November 2013 allows logged-in users to save search results into the "Google Scholar library", a personal collection which the user can search separately and organize by tags.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Via the "metrics" button, it reveals the top journals in a field of interest, and the articles generating these journal's impact can also be accessed. A metrics feature now supports viewing the impact of whole fields of science and academic journals.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Google also included profiles for some posthumous academics, including <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a><sup id="cite&#95;ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Feynman" title="Richard Feynman">Richard Feynman</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> For several years, the profile for <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a><sup id="cite&#95;ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> indicated he was as a "professor at <a href="/wiki/MIT" class="mw-redirect" title="MIT">MIT</a>", with a "verified email at mit.edu".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Features_and_specifications">Features and specifications</h2></div>
<p>Google Scholar allows users to search for digital or physical copies of articles, whether online or in libraries.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It indexes "full-text journal articles, technical reports, preprints, <a href="/wiki/Thesis_or_dissertation" class="mw-redirect" title="Thesis or dissertation">theses</a>, books, and other documents, including selected Web pages that are deemed to be 'scholarly.'"<sup id="cite&#95;ref-pmc1324783&#95;24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmc1324783-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Because many of Google Scholar's search results link to commercial journal articles, most people will be able to access only an abstract and the citation details of an article, and have to pay a fee to access the entire article.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-pmc1324783&#95;24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmc1324783-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The most relevant results for the searched keywords will be listed first, in order of the author's ranking, the number of references that are linked to it and their relevance to other scholarly literature, and the ranking of the publication that the article appears in.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-gsabout&#95;25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gsabout-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Groups_and_access_to_literature">Groups and access to literature</h3></div>
<p>Using its "group of" feature, it shows the available links to journal articles. In the 2005 version, this feature provided a link to both subscription-access versions of an article and to free full-text versions of articles; for most of 2006, it provided links to only the publishers' versions. Since December 2006, it has provided links to both published versions and major open access <a href="/wiki/Repository_(academic_publishing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Repository (academic publishing)">repositories</a>, including all those posted on individual faculty web pages and other unstructured sources identified by similarity. On the other hand, Google Scholar does not allow to filter explicitly between <a href="/wiki/Toll_access" class="mw-redirect" title="Toll access">toll access</a> and <a href="/wiki/Open_access" title="Open access">open access</a> resources, a feature offered <a href="/wiki/Unpaywall" class="mw-redirect" title="Unpaywall">Unpaywall</a> and the tools which embed its data, such as <a href="/wiki/Web_of_Science" title="Web of Science">Web of Science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scopus" title="Scopus">Scopus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Unpaywall_Journals" class="mw-redirect" title="Unpaywall Journals">Unpaywall Journals</a>, used by libraries to calculate the real costs and value of their collections.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Citation_analysis_and_tools">Citation analysis and tools</h3></div>
<p>Through its "cited by" feature, Google Scholar provides access to abstracts of articles that have cited the article being viewed.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-gshelp&#95;27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gshelp-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It is this feature in particular that provides the <a href="/wiki/Citation_indexing" class="mw-redirect" title="Citation indexing">citation indexing</a> previously only found in <a href="/wiki/CiteSeer" class="mw-redirect" title="CiteSeer">CiteSeer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scopus" title="Scopus">Scopus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Web_of_Science" title="Web of Science">Web of Science</a>. Google Scholar also provides links so that citations can be either copied in various formats or imported into user-chosen <a href="/wiki/Reference_management_software" title="Reference management software">reference managers</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Zotero" title="Zotero">Zotero</a>.
</p><p>"Scholar Citations profiles" are public author profiles that are editable by authors themselves.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-profiles2014&#95;16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-profiles2014-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Individuals, logging on through a Google account with a bona fide address usually linked to an academic institution, can now create their own page giving their fields of interest and citations. Google Scholar automatically calculates and displays the individual's total citation count, <a href="/wiki/H-index" title="H-index"><i>h</i>-index</a>, and <a href="/wiki/I10-index" class="mw-redirect" title="I10-index">i10-index</a>. According to Google, "three-quarters of Scholar search results pages ... show links to the authors' public profiles" as of August 2014.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-profiles2014&#95;16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-profiles2014-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Related_articles">Related articles</h3></div>
<p>Through its "Related articles" feature, Google Scholar presents a list of closely related articles, ranked primarily by how similar these articles are to the original result, but also taking into account the relevance of each paper.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="US_legal_case_database">US legal case database</h3></div>
<p>Google Scholar's legal database of US cases is extensive. Users can search and read published opinions of US state appellate and supreme court cases since 1950, US federal district, appellate, tax, and bankruptcy courts since 1923 and US Supreme Court cases since 1791.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-gshelp&#95;27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gshelp-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Google Scholar embeds clickable citation links within the case and the How Cited tab allows lawyers to research prior case law and the subsequent citations to the court decision.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-gsLegalResearch&#95;29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gsLegalResearch-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Ranking_algorithm">Ranking algorithm</h2></div>
<p>While most <a href="/wiki/Academic_databases_and_search_engines" class="mw-redirect" title="Academic databases and search engines">academic databases and search engines</a> allow users to select one factor (e.g. relevance, citation counts, or publication date) to rank results, Google Scholar ranks results with a combined ranking algorithm in a "way researchers do, weighing the full text of each article, the author, the publication in which the article appears, and how often the piece has been cited in other scholarly literature".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-gsabout&#95;25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gsabout-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Research has shown that Google Scholar puts high weight especially on <a href="/wiki/Citation_impact" title="Citation impact">citation counts</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-citcount&#95;30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-citcount-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> as well as words included in a document's title.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In searches by author or year, the first search results are often highly cited articles, as the number of citations is highly determinant, whereas in keyword searches the number of citations is probably the factor with the most weight, but other factors also participate.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Limitations_and_criticism">Limitations and criticism</h2></div>
<p>Some searchers found Google Scholar to be of comparable quality and utility to subscription-based databases when looking at citations of articles in some specific journals.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The reviews recognize that its "cited by" feature in particular poses serious competition to <a href="/wiki/Scopus" title="Scopus">Scopus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Web_of_Science" title="Web of Science">Web of Science</a>. A study looking at the biomedical field found citation information in Google Scholar to be "sometimes inadequate, and less often updated".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The coverage of Google Scholar may vary by discipline compared to other general databases.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>36<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Google Scholar strives to include as many journals as possible, including <a href="/wiki/Predatory_open_access_publishing" class="mw-redirect" title="Predatory open access publishing">predatory journals</a>, which may lack academic rigor. Specialists on predatory journals say that these kinds of journals "have polluted the global scientific record with pseudo-science" and "that Google Scholar dutifully and perhaps blindly includes in its central index."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Google Scholar does not publish a list of journals crawled or publishers included, and the frequency of its updates is uncertain. <a href="/wiki/Bibliometrics" title="Bibliometrics">Bibliometric</a> evidence suggests Google Scholar's coverage of the sciences and social sciences is competitive with other academic databases; as of 2017, Scholar's coverage of the arts and humanities has not been investigated empirically and Scholar's utility for disciplines in these fields remains ambiguous.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Especially early on, some publishers did not allow Scholar to crawl their journals. <a href="/wiki/Elsevier" title="Elsevier">Elsevier</a> journals have been included since mid-2007, when Elsevier began to make most of its <a href="/wiki/ScienceDirect" title="ScienceDirect">ScienceDirect</a> content available to Google Scholar and Google's web search.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However, a 2014 study<sup id="cite&#95;ref-nat&#95;22&#95;5&#95;2014&#95;4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nat_22_5_2014-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> estimates that Google Scholar can find almost 90% (approximately 100 million) of all scholarly documents on the Web written in English. Large-scale longitudinal studies have found between 40 and 60 percent of scientific articles are available in full text via Google Scholar links.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>40<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Google Scholar puts high weight on citation counts in its ranking algorithm and therefore is being criticized for strengthening the <a href="/wiki/Matthew_effect_(sociology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Matthew effect (sociology)">Matthew effect</a>;<sup id="cite&#95;ref-citcount&#95;30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-citcount-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> as highly cited papers appear in top positions they gain more citations while new papers hardly appear in top positions and therefore get less attention by the users of Google Scholar and hence fewer citations. Google Scholar effect is a phenomenon when some researchers pick and cite works appearing in the top results on Google Scholar regardless of their contribution to the citing publication because they automatically assume these works' credibility and believe that editors, reviewers, and readers expect to see these citations.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Google Scholar has problems identifying publications on the <a href="/wiki/ArXiv" title="ArXiv">arXiv</a> preprint server correctly. Interpunctuation characters in titles produce wrong search results, and authors are assigned to wrong papers, which leads to erroneous additional search results. Some search results are even given without any comprehensible reason.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>42<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>43<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Google Scholar is vulnerable to <a href="/wiki/Spam_(electronic)" class="mw-redirect" title="Spam (electronic)">spam</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-spam&#95;44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spam-44"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>44<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-gamingscholar&#95;45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gamingscholar-45"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>45<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Researchers from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Otto-von-Guericke_University_Magdeburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Otto-von-Guericke University Magdeburg">Otto-von-Guericke University Magdeburg</a> demonstrated that citation counts on Google Scholar can be manipulated and complete non-sense articles created with <a href="/wiki/SCIgen" title="SCIgen">SCIgen</a> were indexed within Google Scholar.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:0&#95;46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-46"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>46<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> These researchers concluded that citation counts from Google Scholar should be used with care, especially when used to calculate performance metrics such as the <a href="/wiki/H-index" title="H-index">h-index</a> or <a href="/wiki/Impact_factor" title="Impact factor">impact factor</a>, which is in itself a poor predictor of article quality.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Brembs&#95;2018&#95;47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brembs_2018-47"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>47<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Google Scholar started computing an h-index in 2012 with the advent of individual Scholar pages. Several downstream packages like <i>Harzing's Publish or Perish</i> also use its data.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>48<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The practicality of manipulating h-index calculators by <a href="/wiki/Spoofing_attack" title="Spoofing attack">spoofing</a> Google Scholar was demonstrated in 2010 by Cyril Labbe from <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Fourier_University" title="Joseph Fourier University">Joseph Fourier University</a>, who managed to rank "Ike Antkare" ahead of <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a> by means of a large set of <a href="/wiki/SCIgen" title="SCIgen">SCIgen</a>-produced documents citing each other (effectively an academic <a href="/wiki/Link_farm" title="Link farm">link farm</a>).<sup id="cite&#95;ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>49<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> As of 2010, Google Scholar was not able to <a href="/wiki/Shepardize" class="mw-redirect" title="Shepardize">shepardize</a> case law, as <a href="/wiki/LexisNexis" title="LexisNexis">Lexis</a> could.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>50<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Unlike other indexes of academic work such as <a href="/wiki/Scopus" title="Scopus">Scopus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Web_of_Science" title="Web of Science">Web of Science</a>, Google Scholar does not maintain an <a href="/wiki/Application_Programming_Interface" class="mw-redirect" title="Application Programming Interface">Application Programming Interface</a> that may be used to automate data retrieval. Use of web scrapers to obtain the contents of search results is also severely restricted by the implementation of CAPTCHAs. Google Scholar does not display or export <a href="/wiki/Digital_Object_Identifiers" class="mw-redirect" title="Digital Object Identifiers">Digital Object Identifiers</a> (DOIs),<sup id="cite&#95;ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>51<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> a <i>de facto</i> standard implemented by all major academic publishers to uniquely identify and refer to individual pieces of academic work.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>52<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 2024, researchers found that Google Scholar was manipulatable through citation-purchasing services.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>53<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Search_engine_optimization_for_Google_Scholar">Search engine optimization for Google Scholar</h2></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Search_engine_optimization" title="Search engine optimization">Search engine optimization</a> (SEO) for traditional web search engines such as <a href="/wiki/Google_Search" title="Google Search">Google</a> has been popular for many years. For several years, SEO has also been applied to academic search engines such as Google Scholar.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-54"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>54<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  SEO for <a href="/wiki/Academic_publishing" title="Academic publishing">academic articles</a> is also called "academic search engine optimization" (ASEO) and defined as "the creation, publication, and modification of scholarly literature in a way that makes it easier for academic search engines to both crawl it and index it".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;54-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-54"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>54<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> ASEO has been adopted by several organizations, among them <a href="/wiki/Elsevier" title="Elsevier">Elsevier</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>55<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> OpenScience,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>56<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mendeley" title="Mendeley">Mendeley</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>57<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/SAGE_Publishing" class="mw-redirect" title="SAGE Publishing">SAGE Publishing</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>58<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> to optimize their articles' rankings in Google Scholar. ASEO has been criticised for allowing journals to artificially inflate their metrics and introducing spam into academic search engines.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:0&#95;46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-46"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>46<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
