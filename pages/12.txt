<p><b>Web content</b> is the <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/text" class="extiw" title="wikt:text">text</a>, <a href="/wiki/Visual_system" title="Visual system">visual</a> or <a href="/wiki/Audio_signal" title="Audio signal">audio</a> <a href="/wiki/Content_(media)" class="mw-redirect" title="Content (media)">content</a> that is made available online and user encountered as part of the online usage and <a href="/wiki/User_experience" title="User experience">experience</a> on <a href="/wiki/Website" title="Website">websites</a>. It may include text, <a href="/wiki/Image" title="Image">images</a>, sounds and <a href="/wiki/Digital_audio" title="Digital audio">audio</a>, online videos, among other items placed within <a href="/wiki/Web_page" title="Web page">web pages</a>.
</p><p>In the book <i>Information Architecture for the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a></i>, Lou Rosenfeld and Peter Morville wrote, "We define content broadly as 'the stuff in your website.' Web content may include webpage <a href="/wiki/Electronic_document" title="Electronic document">document</a> pages, information, software data and applications, e-services, images, <a href="/wiki/Audio_signal" title="Audio signal">audio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Video" title="Video">video</a> files, personal Web pages, archived e-mail messages stored on email servers, and more. And we include future web content as well as present web content roadmap."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 219">&#58;&#8202;219&#8202;</span></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Content_management">Content management</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Web_content&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Content management"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1320445320">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}@media print{body.ns-0 .mw-parser-output .hatnote{display:none!important}}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Content_management" title="Content management">Content management</a></div>
<p>Because of websites complexity "<a href="/wiki/Content_management_systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Content management systems">content management systems</a>" started to emerge in the mid 1990s as a tool with which to manage and organize the content on a website.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Content management often means that within a business there is a range of people who have distinct roles to do with content management, such as <a href="/wiki/Writer" title="Writer">content author</a>, <a href="/wiki/Editing" title="Editing">editor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Publishing" title="Publishing">publisher</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Business_administration" title="Business administration">administrator</a>. But it also means there may be a content management system whereby each of the different roles is organized to provide their assistance in operating the system and organizing the information for a website.  A business may also employ various content protection measures, which are typically technologies used to attempt to frustrate copying without permission.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Web_content_accessibility">Web content accessibility</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Web_content&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Web content accessibility"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Web content accessibility is the practice of designing and developing web content that can be used by people with a wide range of disabilities. The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG), developed by the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C), provide comprehensive recommendations structured around four principles: perceivability, operability, understandability, and robustness. These guidelines define success criteria at three levels of conformance (A, AA, and AAA) to help ensure content is accessible across diverse devices and user needs. Common accessibility challenges include providing text alternatives for non-text content, ensuring keyboard navigability, maintaining sufficient color contrast, and creating predictable navigation structures. Adherence to WCAG facilitates legal compliance in many countries and promotes a user-friendly web environment for all users, supporting inclusivity and equal access.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Campoverde2021&#95;3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campoverde2021-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-W3C2025&#95;4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W3C2025-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
