					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-subjectpageheader">
</div><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Software that runs automated tasks on the Internet</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1320445320">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}@media print{body.ns-0 .mw-parser-output .hatnote{display:none!important}}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref">"Wiki bot" redirects here. For bot operation on Wikipedia, see <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Bots" title="Wikipedia:Bots">Wikipedia:Bots</a>.</div>
<p>
An <b>Internet bot</b> (also called a <b>web robot</b> or <b>robot</b>), or simply <b>bot</b>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Etymology,&#95;origin&#95;and&#95;meaning&#95;of&#95;bot&#95;by&#95;etymonline&#95;1922&#95;e339&#95;1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Etymology,_origin_and_meaning_of_bot_by_etymonline_1922_e339-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Software_application" class="mw-redirect" title="Software application">software application</a> that runs automated tasks (<a href="/wiki/Scripting_language" title="Scripting language">scripts</a>) on the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>, usually with the intent to imitate human activity, such as messaging, on a large scale.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:cicero0&#95;2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:cicero0-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> An <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> bot plays the <a href="/wiki/Client_(computing)" title="Client (computing)">client</a> role in a <a href="/wiki/Client%E2%80%93server_model" title="Client–server model">client–server model</a> whereas the <a href="/wiki/Server_(computing)" title="Server (computing)">server</a> role is usually played by <a href="/wiki/Web_server" title="Web server">web servers</a>. Internet bots are able to perform simple and repetitive tasks much faster than a person could ever do. The most extensive use of bots is for <a href="/wiki/Web_crawling" class="mw-redirect" title="Web crawling">web crawling</a>, in which an automated script fetches, analyzes and files information from <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">web</a> servers. More than half of all web traffic is generated by bots.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Zeifman&#95;3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zeifman-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Efforts by web servers to restrict bots vary. Some servers have a <code><a href="/wiki/Robots.txt" title="Robots.txt">robots.txt</a></code> file that contains the rules requesting how bots should behave on website. Any bot that does not follow the rules could, in theory, be denied access to or be removed from the affected <a href="/wiki/Website" title="Website">website</a>.  A website owner cannot force a bot to follow the rules or ensure that a bot's creator or implementer reads or acknowledges the robots.txt file. Some bots are "good", e.g. <a href="/wiki/Search_engine_spider" class="mw-redirect" title="Search engine spider">search engine spiders</a>, while others are used to launch malicious attacks on political campaigns, for example.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Zeifman&#95;3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zeifman-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="IM_and_IRC">IM and IRC</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internet_bot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: IM and IRC"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Some bots communicate with users of Internet-based services, via <a href="/wiki/Instant_messaging" title="Instant messaging">instant messaging</a> (IM), <a href="/wiki/Internet_Relay_Chat" class="mw-redirect" title="Internet Relay Chat">Internet Relay Chat</a> (IRC), or other web interfaces such as <a href="/wiki/Facebook_Messenger" title="Facebook Messenger">Facebook bots</a> and <a href="/wiki/Twitter_bot" title="Twitter bot">Twitter bots</a>. These <a href="/wiki/Chatbot" title="Chatbot">chatbots</a> may allow people to ask questions in plain English and then formulate a response. Such bots can often handle reporting weather, <a href="/wiki/Postal_code" title="Postal code">postal code</a> information, sports scores, currency or other unit conversions, etc.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Others are used for entertainment, such as <a href="/wiki/SmarterChild" title="SmarterChild">SmarterChild</a> on <a href="/wiki/AOL_Instant_Messenger" class="mw-redirect" title="AOL Instant Messenger">AOL Instant Messenger</a> and <a href="/wiki/MSN_Messenger" title="MSN Messenger">MSN Messenger</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Additional roles of an IRC bot may be to listen on a conversation channel, and to comment on certain phrases uttered by the participants (based on <a href="/wiki/Pattern_matching" title="Pattern matching">pattern matching</a>). This is sometimes used as a help service for new users or to censor <a href="/wiki/Profanity" title="Profanity">profanity</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Social_bots">Social bots</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internet_bot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Social bots"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Social_bot" title="Social bot">Social bot</a></div>
<p>Social bots are sets of algorithms that take on the duties of repetitive sets of instructions in order to establish a service or connection among social networking users. Among the various designs of networking bots, the most common are <a href="/wiki/Chat_bot" class="mw-redirect" title="Chat bot">chat bots</a>, algorithms designed to converse with a human user, and social bots, algorithms designed to mimic human behaviors to converse with patterns similar to those of a human user. The history of social botting can be traced back to <a href="/wiki/Alan_Turing" title="Alan Turing">Alan Turing</a> in the 1950s and his vision of designing sets of instructional code approved by the <a href="/wiki/Turing_test" title="Turing test">Turing test</a>. In the 1960s <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Weizenbaum" title="Joseph Weizenbaum">Joseph Weizenbaum</a> created <a href="/wiki/ELIZA" title="ELIZA">ELIZA</a>, a natural language processing computer program considered an early indicator of artificial intelligence algorithms. ELIZA inspired computer programmers to design tasked programs that can match behavior patterns to their sets of instruction. As a result, natural language processing has become an influencing factor to the development of artificial intelligence and social bots. And as information and thought see a progressive mass spreading on social media websites, innovative technological advancements are made following the same pattern.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Twitter_bots_2016-11-13.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Twitter_bots_2016-11-13.png/250px-Twitter_bots_2016-11-13.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="363" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Twitter_bots_2016-11-13.png/500px-Twitter_bots_2016-11-13.png 1.5x" data-file-width="599" data-file-height="869" /></a><figcaption>Twitter bots posting similar messages five days after the <a href="/wiki/2016_United_States_elections" title="2016 United States elections">2016 United States elections</a></figcaption></figure>
<p>Reports of political interferences in recent elections, including the 2016 US and 2017 UK general elections,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> have set the notion of bots being more prevalent because of the ethics that is challenged between the bot's design and the bot's designer. <a href="/wiki/Emilio_Ferrara" title="Emilio Ferrara">Emilio Ferrara</a>, a computer scientist from the University of Southern California reporting on Communications of the ACM,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-RiseOfSocialBots&#95;6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RiseOfSocialBots-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> said the lack of resources available to implement <a href="/wiki/Fact-checking" title="Fact-checking">fact-checking</a> and information verification results in the large volumes of false reports and claims made about these bots on social media platforms. In the case of Twitter, most of these bots are programmed with search filter capabilities that target keywords and phrases favoring political agendas and then retweet them. While the attention of bots is programmed to spread unverified information throughout the social media platforms,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> it is a challenge that programmers face in the wake of a hostile political climate. The Bot Effect is what Ferrera reported as the socialization of bots and human users creating a vulnerability to the leaking of personal information and polarizing influences outside the ethics of the bot's code, and was confirmed by Guillory Kramer in his study where he observed the behavior of emotionally volatile users and the impact the bots have on them, altering their perception of reality.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<p>There has been a great deal of controversy about the use of bots in an automated trading function. Auction website <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">eBay</a> took legal action in an attempt to suppress a third-party company from using bots to look for bargains on its site; this approach backfired on eBay and attracted the attention of further bots. The United Kingdom-based <a href="/wiki/Bet_exchange" class="mw-redirect" title="Bet exchange">bet exchange</a>, <a href="/wiki/Betfair" title="Betfair">Betfair</a>, saw such a large amount of traffic coming from bots that it launched a WebService API aimed at bot programmers, through which it can actively manage bot interactions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Bot farms are known to be used in online app stores, like the <a href="/wiki/Apple_App_Store" class="mw-redirect" title="Apple App Store">Apple App Store</a> and <a href="/wiki/Google_Play" title="Google Play">Google Play</a>, to manipulate positions<sup id="cite&#95;ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> or increase positive ratings/reviews.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>A rapidly growing form of internet bot is the <a href="/wiki/Chatbot" title="Chatbot">chatbot</a>. From 2016, when Facebook Messenger allowed developers to place chatbots on their platform, there has been an exponential growth of their use on that app alone. 30,000 bots were created for Messenger in the first six months, rising to 100,000 by September 2017.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Avi Ben Ezra, CTO of SnatchBot, told Forbes that evidence from the use of their chatbot building platform pointed to a near future saving of millions of hours of human labor as 'live chat' on websites was replaced with bots.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Companies use internet bots to increase online engagement and streamline communication. Companies often use bots to cut down on cost; instead of employing people to communicate with consumers, companies have developed new ways to be efficient. These chatbots are used to answer customers' questions; for example, <a href="/wiki/Domino%27s_Pizza" class="mw-redirect" title="Domino&#39;s Pizza">Domino's</a> developed a chatbot that can take orders via <a href="/wiki/Facebook_Messenger" title="Facebook Messenger">Facebook Messenger</a>. Chatbots allow companies to allocate their employees' time to other tasks.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-MarTech&#95;Today&#95;12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MarTech_Today-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Malicious_bots">Malicious bots</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internet_bot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Malicious bots"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>One example of the malicious use of bots is the coordination and operation of an <a href="/wiki/Automated_threat" title="Automated threat">automated attack</a> on networked computers, such as a <a href="/wiki/Denial-of-service_attack" title="Denial-of-service attack">denial-of-service attack</a> by a <a href="/wiki/Botnet" title="Botnet">botnet</a>. Internet bots or web bots can also be used to commit <a href="/wiki/Click_fraud" title="Click fraud">click fraud</a> and more recently have appeared around <a href="/wiki/MMORPG" class="mw-redirect" title="MMORPG">MMORPG</a> games as <a href="/wiki/Computer_game_bot" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer game bot">computer game bots</a>. Another category is represented by <a href="/wiki/Spambot" title="Spambot">spambots</a>, internet bots that attempt to <a href="/wiki/Spam_(electronic)" class="mw-redirect" title="Spam (electronic)">spam</a> large amounts of content on the Internet, usually adding advertising links. More than 94.2% of websites have experienced a bot attack.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Zeifman&#95;3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zeifman-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>There are malicious bots (and <a href="/wiki/Botnets" class="mw-redirect" title="Botnets">botnets</a>) of the following types:
</p>
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Spambot" title="Spambot">Spambots</a> that harvest email addresses from contact or guestbook pages</li>
<li>Downloaded programs that suck <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(computing)" title="Bandwidth (computing)">bandwidth</a> by downloading entire websites</li>
<li>Website scrapers that grab the content of websites and re-use it without permission on automatically generated doorway pages</li>
<li>Registration bots that sign up a specific email address to numerous services in order to have the confirmation messages flood the email inbox and distract from important messages indicating a security breach.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Bekerman&#95;13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bekerman-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Viruses and worms</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/DDoS" class="mw-redirect" title="DDoS">DDoS</a> attacks</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Botnets" class="mw-redirect" title="Botnets">Botnets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zombie_(computer_science)" class="mw-redirect" title="Zombie (computer science)">zombie computers</a>, etc.</li>
<li>Spambots that try to redirect people onto a malicious website, sometimes found in comment sections or forums of various websites</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Viewbot" title="Viewbot">Viewbots</a> create fake views<sup id="cite&#95;ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Bots that buy up high-demand seats for concerts, operated by <a href="/wiki/Ticket_brokers" class="mw-redirect" title="Ticket brokers">ticket brokers</a> who resell the tickets.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> These bots run through the purchase process of entertainment event-ticketing sites and obtain as many seats as they can.</li>
<li>Bots that are used in <a href="/wiki/Massively_multiplayer_online_role-playing_game" title="Massively multiplayer online role-playing game">massively multiplayer online role-playing games</a> to farm for resources that would otherwise take significant time or effort to obtain, which can be a concern for online in-game economies.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Bots that increase traffic counts on analytics reporting to extract money from advertisers. A study by <a href="/wiki/Comscore" title="Comscore">Comscore</a> found that over half of ads shown across thousands of campaigns between May 2012 and February 2013 were not served to human users.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Bots used on internet forums to automatically post inflammatory or nonsensical posts to <a href="/wiki/Internet_troll" class="mw-redirect" title="Internet troll">disrupt the forum and anger users</a>.</li></ol>
<p>in 2012, journalist Percy von Lipinski reported that he discovered millions of bots or botted or pinged views at CNN <a href="/wiki/IReport" title="IReport">iReport</a>. <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">CNN</a> iReport quietly removed millions of views from the account of iReporter Chris Morrow.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It is not known if the ad revenue received by CNN from the fake views was ever returned to the advertisers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The most widely used anti-bot technique is <a href="/wiki/CAPTCHA" title="CAPTCHA">CAPTCHA</a>. Examples of providers include <a href="/wiki/Recaptcha" class="mw-redirect" title="Recaptcha">Recaptcha</a>, Minteye, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solve_Media&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Solve Media (page does not exist)">Solve Media</a> and NuCaptcha. However, captchas are not foolproof in preventing bots, as they can often be <a href="/wiki/CAPTCHA#Circumvention" title="CAPTCHA">circumvented</a> by computer character recognition, security holes, and outsourcing captcha solving to cheap laborers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Protection_against_bots">Protection against bots</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internet_bot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Protection against bots"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In the case of academic surveys, protection against auto test taking bots is essential for maintaining accuracy and consistency in the results of the survey. Without proper precautions against these bots, the results of a survey can become skewed or inaccurate. Researchers indicate that the best way to keep bots out of surveys is to not allow them to enter to begin with. The survey should have participants from a reliable source, such as an existing department or group at work. This way, malicious bots don't have the opportunity to infiltrate the study.
</p><p>Another form of protection against bots is a CAPTCHA test as mentioned in a previous section, which stands for "Completely Automated Public Turing Test". &#160;This test is often used to quickly distinguish a real user from a bot by posing a challenge that a human could easily do but a bot would not. &#160;This could be something like recognizing distorted letters or numbers, or picking out specific parts of an image, such as traffic lights on a busy street. CAPTCHAs are a great form of protection due to their ability to be completed quickly, low effort, and easy implementation.
</p><p>There are also dedicated companies that specialize in protection against bots, including ones like DataDome, Akamai and Imperva. &#160;These companies offer defense systems to their clients to protect them against DDoS attacks, infrastructure attacks, and overall cybersecurity. &#160;While the pricing rates of these companies can often be expensive, the services offered can be crucial both for large corporations and small businesses.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Human_interaction_with_social_bots">Human interaction with social bots</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internet_bot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Human interaction with social bots"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1305433154" /><table class="box-Unbalanced&#95;section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><span class="skin-invert-image" typeof="mw:File"><span><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Unbalanced_scales.svg/60px-Unbalanced_scales.svg.png" decoding="async" width="45" height="40" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Unbalanced_scales.svg/120px-Unbalanced_scales.svg.png 1.5x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="354" /></span></span></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>may be <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:DUE" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:DUE">unbalanced</a> towards certain viewpoints</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Internet_bot&amp;action=edit">improve the article</a> or discuss the issue on the <a href="/wiki/Talk:Internet_bot" title="Talk:Internet bot">talk page</a>.</span>  <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">November 2021</span>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>There are two main concerns with bots: clarity and face-to-face support. The cultural background of human beings affects the way they communicate with social bots.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Others recognize that online bots have the ability to "masquerade" as humans online and have become highly aware of their presence. Due to this, some users are becoming unsure when interacting with a social bot.
</p><p>Many people believe that bots are vastly less intelligent than humans, so they are not worthy of our respect.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:cicero0&#95;2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:cicero0-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Min-Sun Kim proposed five concerns or issues that may arise when communicating with a social robot, and they are avoiding the damage of peoples' feelings, minimizing impositions, disapproval from others, clarity issues, and how effective their messages may come across.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:cicero0&#95;2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:cicero0-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>People who oppose social robots argue that they also take away from the genuine creations of human relationships.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:cicero0&#95;2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:cicero0-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Opposition to social bots also note that the use of social bots add a new, unnecessary layer to privacy protection. Many users call for stricter legislation in relation to social bots to ensure private information remains preserved. The discussion of what to do with social bots and how far they should go remains ongoing.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Social_bots_and_political_discussions">Social bots and political discussions</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internet_bot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Social bots and political discussions"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In recent years, political discussion platforms and politics on social media have become highly unstable and volatile. With the introduction of social bots on the political discussion scene, many users worry about their effect on the discussion and election outcomes. The biggest offender on the social media side is X (previously Twitter), where heated political discussions are raised both by bots and real users. The result is a misuse of political discussion on these platforms and a general mistrust among users for what they see.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2025)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
