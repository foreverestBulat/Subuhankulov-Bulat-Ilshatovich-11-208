<p><b>Clyde Lee Giles</b> is an American computer scientist and is the Emeritus David Reese Professor at the <a href="/wiki/Penn_State_College_of_Information_Sciences_and_Technology" class="mw-redirect" title="Penn State College of Information Sciences and Technology">Penn State College of Information Sciences and Technology</a> (IST) at the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_State_University" title="Pennsylvania State University">Pennsylvania State University</a> where he taught for 24 years. He was also Graduate Faculty Professor of Computer Science and Engineering, Courtesy Professor of Supply Chain and Information Systems, and Director of the Intelligent Systems Research Laboratory. He was Interim Associate Dean of Research in the College of IST. He is now emeritus faculty. He graduated from Oakhaven High School in Memphis, Tennessee.  His graduate degrees are from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Michigan" title="University of Michigan">University of Michigan</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Arizona" title="University of Arizona">University of Arizona</a> and his undergraduate degrees are from <a href="/wiki/Rhodes_College" title="Rhodes College">Rhodes College</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tennessee" title="University of Tennessee">University of Tennessee</a>. His PhD is in optical sciences with advisor Harrison H. Barrett. His <a href="/wiki/Academic_genealogy" title="Academic genealogy">academic genealogy</a> includes two Nobel laureates (<a href="/wiki/Felix_Bloch" title="Felix Bloch">Felix Bloch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg" title="Werner Heisenberg">Werner Heisenberg</a>), <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Sommerfeld" title="Arnold Sommerfeld">Arnold Sommerfeld</a> and prominent mathematicians.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Research/Career"><span id="Research.2FCareer"></span>Research/Career</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lee_Giles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Research/Career"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Giles has been associated with the computer science or electrical engineering departments at <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a>, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pisa" title="University of Pisa">University of Pisa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Trento" title="University of Trento">University of Trento</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Maryland,_College_Park" title="University of Maryland, College Park">University of Maryland, College Park</a>. Previous positions were at NEC Research Institute (now NEC Labs), Princeton, NJ; <a href="/wiki/Air_Force_Research_Laboratory" title="Air Force Research Laboratory">Air Force Research Laboratory</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Naval_Research_Laboratory" title="United States Naval Research Laboratory">United States Naval Research Laboratory</a>. He is best known for his work on the creation of novel scientific and academic search engines and digital libraries and is considered by some one of the founders of academic document search. Earlier research was concerned with <a href="/wiki/Recurrent_neural_networks" class="mw-redirect" title="Recurrent neural networks">recurrent neural networks</a> and optical computing.
</p><p>His research interests are in intelligent web and cyberinfrastructure tools, search engines and information retrieval, digital libraries, web services, knowledge and information management and extraction, machine learning, and information and data mining. He has created several <a href="/wiki/Vertical_search" title="Vertical search">vertical search</a> engines in these areas. He has over 500 publications with some in <a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)"><i>Nature</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)"><i>Science</i></a> and the <a href="/wiki/PNAS" class="mw-redirect" title="PNAS"><i>Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences</i></a>. His research is well cited with an <a href="/wiki/H-index" title="H-index">h-index</a> of 118 according to Google Scholar and over 60,000 total citations as evidenced in <a href="/wiki/Google_Scholar" title="Google Scholar">Google Scholar</a>.  He has one of the top 200 h-indexes in Computer Science and the top 10 in Information Retrieval. At Penn State he has graduated 40 PHD students.
Most of his papers author his name as C. Lee Giles or C.L. Giles.
</p><p>Most of his classes were graduate courses on deep learning and graduate/upperclassman courses on search engines and information retrieval.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Awards">Awards</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lee_Giles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Awards"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>He is a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="Association for Computing Machinery">Association for Computing Machinery</a> (ACM),<sup id="cite&#95;ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers (<a href="/wiki/IEEE" class="mw-redirect" title="IEEE">IEEE</a>),<sup id="cite&#95;ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Neural_Network_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="International Neural Network Society (page does not exist)">International Neural Network Society</a> (INNS). He also received the <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Gabor" title="Dennis Gabor">Gabor</a> Award<sup id="cite&#95;ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> from the International Neural Network Society (INNS) recognizing achievements in engineering/applications in neural networks.  Most recently he received the 2018 Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers (IEEE) Computational Intelligence Society (CIS) Neural Networks Pioneer Award<sup id="cite&#95;ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and the 2018 National Federation of Advanced Information Services (NFAIS) Miles Conrad Award.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>He has twice received the IBM Distinguished Faculty Award.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Recently, he was recognized as one of the 100 worldwide academic leaders in data by CDO, the Chief Data Officer Magazine. 
<sup id="cite&#95;ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Electromagnetics">Electromagnetics</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lee_Giles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Electromagnetics"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Before his work on neural networks, Giles published papers on reflection and scattering of electromagnetic waves from magnetic materials for the particular cases of equal refractive indexes. His work is mentioned in the following articles: <a href="/wiki/Fresnel_equations" title="Fresnel equations">Fresnel equations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mie_scattering" title="Mie scattering">Mie scattering</a>, and  <a href="/wiki/Brewster%27s_angle" title="Brewster&#39;s angle">Brewster's angle</a>. For <a href="/wiki/Mie_scattering" title="Mie scattering">Mie scattering</a>, he is a coauthor on the Kerker effect, which was an extension of his work on a planar boundary effect and his idea.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Neural_networks">Neural networks</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lee_Giles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Neural networks"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Giles' work on neural networks showed that fundamental computational structures such as regular grammars and finite state machines could be theoretically represented in recurrent neural networks. Another contribution was the Neural Network Pushdown Automata and the first analog differentiable stack. Some of these publications are cited as early work in "deep" learning.
</p><p>While at AFOSR in Washington DC, Giles in 1986 established the first neural network funding in 20 years and helped DARPA establish theirs.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="CiteSeer_and_search_engines">CiteSeer and search engines</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lee_Giles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: CiteSeer and search engines"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In 1998 and 1999 his work published in <i>Science and Nature</i> with Steve Lawrence estimated the size of the web and showed that search engines did not index that much of it. This work also showed that the web had significantly matured and had a diversity of material and resources.
</p><p>With <a href="/wiki/Steve_Lawrence_(computer_scientist)" title="Steve Lawrence (computer scientist)">Steve Lawrence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Bollacker" title="Kurt Bollacker">Kurt Bollacker</a>, Giles was responsible for the creation in 1997 of automatic citation indexing<sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/CiteSeer" class="mw-redirect" title="CiteSeer">CiteSeer</a>, a public academic search engine and digital library for Computer and Information Science. Under his direction <a href="/wiki/CiteSeer" class="mw-redirect" title="CiteSeer">CiteSeer</a> was moved to and is being maintained at the Pennsylvania State University. CiteSeer has been replaced by the Next Generation CiteSeer, <a href="/wiki/CiteSeerX" title="CiteSeerX">CiteSeerX</a>.
</p><p>He is the director of the Next Generation CiteSeer project, <a href="/wiki/CiteSeerX" title="CiteSeerX">CiteSeerX</a>, also at the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_State_University" title="Pennsylvania State University">Pennsylvania State University</a>. In addition, he was responsible for the creation of an academic business search engine and digital library, <a href="/wiki/BizSeer" class="mw-redirect" title="BizSeer">BizSeer</a> (previously known as SmealSearch). With <a href="/w/index.php?title=Isaac_Councill&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Isaac Councill (page does not exist)">Isaac Councill</a>, he created automatic <a href="/wiki/Acknowledgement_index" class="mw-redirect" title="Acknowledgement index">acknowledgement indexing</a>, permitting for the first time the automatic search and indexing of acknowledged entities in scholarly and research documents. The search engine for this was AckSeer. He also was the cocreator of the first search engine for robots.txt, <a href="/wiki/BotSeer" title="BotSeer">BotSeer</a>.
</p><p>Research in collaboration with Professors Prasenjit Mitra, Karl Mueller, Barbara Garrison and James Kubicki resulted in the development of a search engine and data portal for chemistry, Chem<sub>x</sub>Seer, <a href="/wiki/ChemXSeer" title="ChemXSeer">ChemXSeer</a>. With Yang Sun, a novel search engine, <a href="/wiki/BotSeer" title="BotSeer">BotSeer</a>, was designed that searches and indexes <a href="/wiki/Robots.txt" title="Robots.txt">robots.txt</a> files on web sites. The Next Generation CiteSeer, CiteSeer<sup>x</sup>, came online in February 2008, with over one million articles indexed and now with active crawling exceeds 10 million articles. <a href="/w/index.php?title=RefSeerX&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="RefSeerX (page does not exist)">RefSeerX</a> was a context-aware citation recommendation service which recommended papers from <a href="/wiki/CiteSeerX" title="CiteSeerX">CiteSeerX</a> that are most relevant to a given text. These services were based on SeerSuite, a package of open sources tools for searching and indexing academic documents and data.
</p>
