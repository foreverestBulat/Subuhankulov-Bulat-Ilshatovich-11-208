<p><b>Edward Grady "Ed" Coffman Jr.</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Computer_scientist" title="Computer scientist">computer scientist</a>. He began his career as a systems programmer at the <a href="/wiki/System_Development_Corporation" title="System Development Corporation">System Development Corporation</a> (SDC) during the period 1958–65. His <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Engineering" title="Doctor of Engineering">PhD in engineering</a> at <a href="/wiki/UCLA" class="mw-redirect" title="UCLA">UCLA</a> in 1966 was followed by a series of positions at <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a> (1966–69), The <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_State_University" title="Pennsylvania State University">Pennsylvania State University</a> (1970–76), <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> (1976–77), and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Santa_Barbara" title="University of California, Santa Barbara">University of California, Santa Barbara</a> (1977–79).  In 1979, he joined the Mathematics Center at <a href="/wiki/Bell_Laboratories" class="mw-redirect" title="Bell Laboratories">Bell Laboratories</a> where he stayed until his retirement as a Distinguished Member of Technical Staff 20 years later. After a one-year stint at the <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey_Institute_of_Technology" title="New Jersey Institute of Technology">New Jersey Institute of Technology</a>, he returned to <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> in 2000 with appointments in <a href="/wiki/Computer_Science" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer Science">Computer Science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrical_Engineering" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical Engineering">Electrical Engineering</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Engineering_and_Operations_Research" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial Engineering and Operations Research">Industrial Engineering and Operations Research</a>.  He retired from teaching in 2008 and is now a professor emeritus still engaged in research and professional activities.
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Research">Research</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_G._Coffman_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Research"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Coffman is best known for his seminal research together with his international collaborations, measured in part by some 150 co-authors in his collection of publications.  His work can be found in over 180 articles in technical journals devoted to original research contributions. He published 4 graduate-level text books, and papers in the proceedings of some 250 conferences and workshops, most of these being preliminary versions of journal articles.  In his research, Coffman has been a generalist following many parallel paths in engineering and applied mathematics. The directions he has taken have drawn on the tools of combinatorial optimization and the theory of algorithms, along with those of applied probability and stochastic processes. The processes studied include those in the theories of <a href="/wiki/Scheduling_(computing)" title="Scheduling (computing)">scheduling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bin_packing" class="mw-redirect" title="Bin packing">bin packing</a>, sequential selection, <a href="/wiki/Graph_algorithms" class="mw-redirect" title="Graph algorithms">graphs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_allocation" class="mw-redirect" title="Dynamic allocation">dynamic allocation</a>, along with those in <a href="/wiki/Queueing_theory" title="Queueing theory">queueing</a>, polling, reservation, <a href="/wiki/K-server_problem" title="K-server problem">moving-server</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer_network" title="Computer network">networking</a>, and distributed <a href="/w/index.php?title=Local-rule_systems&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Local-rule systems (page does not exist)">local-rule systems</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Cellular_automaton" title="Cellular automaton">cellular automata</a>). His contributions have been divided between mathematical foundations and the design and analysis of <a href="/wiki/Approximation_algorithms" class="mw-redirect" title="Approximation algorithms">approximation algorithms</a> providing the basis for engineering solutions to <a href="/wiki/NP-hard" class="mw-redirect" title="NP-hard">NP-hard</a> problems. Computer and network engineering applications have been broad in scope; a partial list includes research addressing problems in the scheduling and storage allocation functions of computer <a href="/wiki/Operating_systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Operating systems">operating systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer_storage" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer storage">storage architectures</a>, <a href="/wiki/Data_structures" class="mw-redirect" title="Data structures">data structures</a>, computer timing problems such as <a href="/wiki/Deadlock_(computer_science)" title="Deadlock (computer science)">deadlocks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Synchronization_(computer_science)" title="Synchronization (computer science)">synchronization</a>, Internet congestion, <a href="/wiki/Peer-to-peer_file_sharing" title="Peer-to-peer file sharing">peer-to-peer file sharing</a> networks, stream merging, <a href="/wiki/Self-assembly" title="Self-assembly">self-assembly</a> processes of <a href="/wiki/Dna_computing" class="mw-redirect" title="Dna computing">molecular computing</a>, minimalist algorithms in <a href="/wiki/Sensor_networks" class="mw-redirect" title="Sensor networks">sensor networks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Optical_burst_switching" title="Optical burst switching">optical burst switching</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_Spectrum_Management" class="mw-redirect" title="Dynamic Spectrum Management">dynamic spectrum management</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_networks" class="mw-redirect" title="Cognitive networks">cognitive networks</a>.  The list expands greatly when including the myriad applications in <a href="/wiki/Industrial_engineering_and_operations_research" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial engineering and operations research">industrial engineering and operations research</a> of Coffman's research in scheduling and bin-packing theory in one and two dimensions. As of 11 November 2015, his works have been cited 13,597 times, and he has an <a href="/wiki/H-index" title="H-index">h-index</a> of 55.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Coffman has been active professionally serving on several editorial boards, dozens of technical program committees, setting research agendas in workshops of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Research_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="United States National Research Council">National Research Council</a>, co-founding the <a href="/wiki/Symposium_on_Operating_Systems_Principles" title="Symposium on Operating Systems Principles">Symposium on Operating Systems Principles</a>, and the special interest groups on performance evaluation of both <a href="/wiki/ACM_SIGMETRICS" class="mw-redirect" title="ACM SIGMETRICS">ACM</a> and <a href="/wiki/IFIPS" class="mw-redirect" title="IFIPS">IFIPS</a>.
</p>
