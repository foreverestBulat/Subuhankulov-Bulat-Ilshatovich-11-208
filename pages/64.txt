</div><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Reduced-size versions of images or videos</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1320445320">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}@media print{body.ns-0 .mw-parser-output .hatnote{display:none!important}}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For the body part, see <a href="/wiki/Nail_(anatomy)" title="Nail (anatomy)">Nail (anatomy)</a>. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Thumbnail_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Thumbnail (disambiguation)">Thumbnail (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Anzeige_Miniaturansicht_auf_commons_Kategorien_Unterkategorien_analog_Windows-Explorer_(cropped).jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Anzeige_Miniaturansicht_auf_commons_Kategorien_Unterkategorien_analog_Windows-Explorer_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Anzeige_Miniaturansicht_auf_commons_Kategorien_Unterkategorien_analog_Windows-Explorer_%28cropped%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="136" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d7/Anzeige_Miniaturansicht_auf_commons_Kategorien_Unterkategorien_analog_Windows-Explorer_%28cropped%29.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="252" data-file-height="137" /></a><figcaption>Thumbnail images being used to show a sample of image files within a folder, on a computer operating system.</figcaption></figure>
<p>A <b>thumbnail</b> is a reduced-size version of a picture or <a href="/wiki/Video" title="Video">video</a>, used to help in recognizing and organizing them, serving the same role for images as a normal text <a href="/wiki/Index_(publishing)" title="Index (publishing)">index</a> does for words.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In the age of <a href="/wiki/Digital_image" title="Digital image">digital images</a>, visual <a href="/wiki/Search_engine" title="Search engine">search engines</a> and image-organizing programs normally use thumbnails, as do most modern operating systems or desktop environments, such as <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a>, <a href="/wiki/MacOS" title="MacOS">macOS</a>, <a href="/wiki/KDE" title="KDE">KDE</a> (<a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a>) and <a href="/wiki/GNOME" title="GNOME">GNOME</a> (<a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a>). On web pages, they also avoid the need to download larger files unnecessarily.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Implementation">Implementation</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thumbnail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Implementation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Thumbnails are ideally implemented on <a href="/wiki/Web_page" title="Web page">web pages</a> as separate, smaller copies of the original image, in part because one purpose of a thumbnail image on a web page is to reduce <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(computing)" title="Bandwidth (computing)">bandwidth</a> and download time.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Some <a href="/wiki/Web_design" title="Web design">web designers</a> produce thumbnails with <a href="/wiki/HTML_element" title="HTML element">HTML</a> or <a href="/wiki/Client-side" class="mw-redirect" title="Client-side">client-side</a> scripting that makes the user's browser shrink the picture, rather than use a smaller copy of the image. This results in no saved bandwidth, and the visual quality of browser resizing is usually less than ideal.
</p><p>Displaying a significant part of the picture instead of the full frame can allow the use of a smaller thumbnail while maintaining recognizability. For example, when thumbnailing a full-body <a href="/wiki/Portrait" title="Portrait">portrait</a> of a person, it may be better to show the face slightly reduced than an indistinct figure. However, this may <a href="/wiki/Deception#Online_disinhibition" title="Deception">mislead</a> the viewer about what the image contains, so is more suited to artistic presentations than searching or catalogue browsing.
</p><p>Thumbnail makes for smaller, more easily viewable pages and also allows viewers to have control over exactly what they want to see.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In 2002, the court in the US case <i><a href="/wiki/Kelly_v._Arriba_Soft_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Kelly v. Arriba Soft Corporation">Kelly v. Arriba Soft Corporation</a></i> ruled that it was <a href="/wiki/Fair_use" title="Fair use">fair use</a> for <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> search engines to use thumbnail images to help web users find what they seek.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Etymology">Etymology</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thumbnail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The word <i>thumbnail</i> is a reference to the human thumbnail and alludes to the small size of an image or picture, comparable to the size of the nail.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Online&#95;Etymology&#95;Dictionary&#95;5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Online_Etymology_Dictionary-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-AllWords.com&#95;6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllWords.com-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The earliest use of the compound word <i>thumbnail</i> in the literal sense dates back to at least the 17th century;<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Random&#95;House&#95;Word&#95;of&#95;the&#95;Day&#95;7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Random_House_Word_of_the_Day-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> the <i>American Heritage Dictionary of Idioms</i> documents that the figurative expression first appeared in the mid-19th century to refer to "a drawing the size of the thumbnail".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Podictionary&#95;8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Podictionary-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The word was then used figuratively, in both <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">noun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adjective</a> form, to refer to anything small or concise, such as a biographical essay. The use of the word <i>thumbnail</i> in the specific context of computer images as "a small graphical representation, as of a larger graphic, a page layout, etc." appears to have been first used in the 1980s.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Random&#95;House&#95;Word&#95;of&#95;the&#95;Day&#95;7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Random_House_Word_of_the_Day-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Dimensions">Dimensions</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thumbnail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dimensions"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Denver_Public_Library" title="Denver Public Library">Denver Public Library</a> Digitization and Cataloguing Program produces thumbnails that are 160 <a href="/wiki/Pixel" title="Pixel">pixels</a> in the long dimension.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/California_Digital_Library" title="California Digital Library">California Digital Library</a> Guidelines for Digital Images recommend 150-200 pixels for each dimension.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Picture_Australia" class="mw-redirect" title="Picture Australia">Picture Australia</a> requires thumbnails to be 150 pixels in the long dimension.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/International_Dunhuang_Project" class="mw-redirect" title="International Dunhuang Project">International Dunhuang Project</a> Standards for Digitization and Image Management specifies a height of 96 pixels at 72 <a href="/wiki/Pixels_per_inch" class="mw-redirect" title="Pixels per inch">ppi</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> recommends the resolution of 1280×720 (with a minimum width of 640 pixels) with an aspect ratio of 16:9.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/DeviantArt" title="DeviantArt">DeviantArt</a> automatically produces thumbnails that are maximum 150 pixels in the long dimension.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Flickr" title="Flickr">Flickr</a> automatically produces thumbnails that are a maximum 240 pixels in the long dimension, or smaller 75×75 pixels. It also applies <a href="/wiki/Unsharp_mask" class="mw-redirect" title="Unsharp mask">unsharp mask</a> to them.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Picasa" title="Picasa">Picasa</a> automatically produces thumbnails that are a maximum 144 pixels in the long dimension, or 160×160 pixels album thumbnails.</li></ul>
<p>The term <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vignette" class="extiw" title="wikt:vignette">vignette</a></i> is sometimes used to describe an image that is smaller than the original, larger than a thumbnail, but no more than 250 pixels in the long dimension.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Art_director" title="Art director">Directors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Storyboard_artist" title="Storyboard artist">storyboard artists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Graphic_design" title="Graphic design">graphic designers</a>, as well as other kinds of visual artists, use the term "thumbnail sketch" to describe a small drawing on paper (usually part of a group) used to explore multiple ideas quickly. Thumbnail sketches are similar to <a href="/wiki/Doodle" title="Doodle">doodles</a>, but may include as  much detail as a small <a href="/wiki/Pencil_sketching" class="mw-redirect" title="Pencil sketching">sketch</a>. A "comprehensive" thumbnail sketch of a printed project, more or less to final size, is often referred to as a "comp", and can be highly detailed, with production information included. The purpose of thumbnails was to visualize the ideas in a miniature form, similar to an illustration shorthand. Often, the old school animators used this process to quickly jot down the key "poses" that were part of an animation sequence. These compact drawing were then pinned up above the animation table, within easy view. As the animator worked through creating the final drawings of each pose, the thumbnails helped to keep the original ideation relevant.
</p>
