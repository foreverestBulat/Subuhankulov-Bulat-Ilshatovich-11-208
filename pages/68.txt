<p><b>WebCrawler</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Search_engine" title="Search engine">search engine</a>, and one of the oldest surviving search engines on the web today. For many years, it operated as a <a href="/wiki/Metasearch_engine" title="Metasearch engine">metasearch engine</a>. WebCrawler was the first web search engine to provide <a href="/wiki/Full-text_search" title="Full-text search">full-text search</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:0&#95;1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>

<p>Brian Pinkerton first started working on WebCrawler, which was originally a desktop application, on January 27, 1994 at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Washington" title="University of Washington">University of Washington</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> On March 15, 1994, he generated a list of the top 25 websites.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:0&#95;1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>WebCrawler launched on April 21, 1994, with more than 4,000 different websites in its database<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and on November 14, 1994, WebCrawler served its 1 millionth search query<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> for "nuclear weapons design and research".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>On December 1, 1994, WebCrawler acquired two sponsors, <a href="/w/index.php?title=DealerNet&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="DealerNet (page does not exist)">DealerNet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Starwave" title="Starwave">Starwave</a>, which provided money to keep WebCrawler operating.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Starting on October 3, 1995, WebCrawler was fully supported by advertising, but separated the adverts from search results.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>On June 1, 1995, <a href="/wiki/America_Online" class="mw-redirect" title="America Online">America Online</a> (AOL) acquired WebCrawler.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> After being acquired by AOL, the website introduced its mascot "Spidey" on September 1, 1995.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Starting in April 1996,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> WebCrawler also included the human-edited internet guide <a href="/wiki/Global_Network_Navigator" title="Global Network Navigator">GNN Select</a>, which was also under AOL ownership.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>On April 1, 1997, <a href="/wiki/Excite_(web_portal)" title="Excite (web portal)">Excite</a> acquired WebCrawler from AOL for $12.3 million.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>WebCrawler received a redesign on June 16, 1997, adding WebCrawler Shortcuts, which suggested alternative links to material related to a search topic.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>WebCrawler was maintained by Excite as a separate search engine with its own database until 2001, when it started using Excite's own database, effectively putting an end to WebCrawler as an independent search engine.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Later that year, Excite (then called <a href="/wiki/Excite@Home" class="mw-redirect" title="Excite@Home">Excite@Home</a>) went bankrupt and WebCrawler was bought by <a href="/wiki/InfoSpace" title="InfoSpace">InfoSpace</a> in 2001.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:WebCrawler_Screenshot_6-7-2010.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/21/WebCrawler_Screenshot_6-7-2010.png/250px-WebCrawler_Screenshot_6-7-2010.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="174" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/21/WebCrawler_Screenshot_6-7-2010.png/378px-WebCrawler_Screenshot_6-7-2010.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/2/21/WebCrawler_Screenshot_6-7-2010.png 2x" data-file-width="378" data-file-height="263" /></a><figcaption>WebCrawler's homepage (June 2010)</figcaption></figure>
<p>Pinkerton, WebCrawler's creator, led the Amazon <a href="/wiki/A9.com" title="A9.com">A9.com</a> search division as of 2012.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-before&#95;9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-before-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In July 2016, InfoSpace was sold by parent company <a href="/wiki/Blucora" class="mw-redirect" title="Blucora">Blucora</a> to <a href="/wiki/OpenMail" class="mw-redirect" title="OpenMail">OpenMail</a> for $45 million, putting WebCrawler under the ownership of OpenMail.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> OpenMail was later renamed <a href="/wiki/System1" title="System1">System1</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In 2018, WebCrawler was redesigned from scratch and its logo was changed.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Traffic">Traffic</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WebCrawler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Traffic"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>WebCrawler was highly successful early on.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> At one point, it was unusable during peak times due to server overload.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It was the second most visited website on the internet in February 1996, but it quickly dropped below rival search engines and directories such as <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!" class="mw-redirect" title="Yahoo!">Yahoo!</a>, <a href="/wiki/Infoseek" title="Infoseek">Infoseek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lycos" title="Lycos">Lycos</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Excite_(web_portal)" title="Excite (web portal)">Excite</a> in 1997.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
