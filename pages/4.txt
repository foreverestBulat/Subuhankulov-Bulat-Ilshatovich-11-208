					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-subjectpageheader">
</div><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Intentional or unintentional release of secure information</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p><p>A <b>data breach</b>, also known as <b>data leakage</b>, is "the unauthorized exposure, disclosure, or loss of <a href="/wiki/Personal_information" class="mw-redirect" title="Personal information">personal information</a>".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog20225&#95;1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog20225-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Attackers have a variety of motives, from financial gain to <a href="/wiki/Hacktivism" title="Hacktivism">political activism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Political_repression" title="Political repression">political repression</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Espionage" title="Espionage">espionage</a>. There are several technical root causes of data breaches, including accidental or intentional disclosure of information by insiders, loss or theft of <a href="/wiki/Encryption" title="Encryption">unencrypted</a> devices, hacking into a system by exploiting <a href="/wiki/Software_vulnerabilities" class="mw-redirect" title="Software vulnerabilities">software vulnerabilities</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Social_engineering_attack" class="mw-redirect" title="Social engineering attack">social engineering attacks</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Phishing" title="Phishing">phishing</a> where insiders are tricked into disclosing information. Although prevention efforts can reduce the risk of a data breach, they cannot eliminate it.
</p><p>A large number of data breaches are never detected. If a breach becomes known to the company holding the data, post-breach efforts commonly include containing the breach, investigating its scope and cause, and notifications to people whose records were compromised, as required by law in many jurisdictions. Law enforcement agencies may investigate breaches, although the hackers responsible are rarely caught.
</p><p>Criminals often sell data obtained in breaches on the <a href="/wiki/Dark_web" title="Dark web">dark web</a>. Thus, people whose personal data was compromised are at elevated risk of <a href="/wiki/Identity_theft" title="Identity theft">identity theft</a> for years afterwards and a significant number will become victims of this crime. <a href="/wiki/Data_breach_notification_laws" title="Data breach notification laws">Data breach notification laws</a> in many jurisdictions, including all <a href="/wiki/States_of_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="States of the United States">states of the United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/European_Union_member_states" class="mw-redirect" title="European Union member states">European Union member states</a>, require the notification of people whose data has been breached. Lawsuits against the company that was breached are common, although few victims receive money from them. There is little empirical evidence of economic harm to firms from breaches except the direct cost, although there is some evidence suggesting a temporary, short-term decline in <a href="/wiki/Share_price" title="Share price">stock price</a>.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Definition">Definition</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Data_breach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Like almost all terms in cyber security, the definition of "data breach" is context-dependent. 
</p>
<ul><li>According to the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">National Institute of Standards and Technology</a> (NIST), a data breach is "An occurrence that actually or potentially jeopardizes the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of an information system or that constitutes a violation or imminent threat of violating security policies, security procedures, or acceptable use policies.".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>The UK National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC) defines a data breach as occurring "when information held by an organisation is stolen or accessed without authorisation".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Others define a data breach as a violation of "organizational, regulatory, legislative or contractual" law or policy<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler20162&#95;4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler20162-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> that causes "the unauthorized exposure, disclosure, or loss of <a href="/wiki/Personal_information" class="mw-redirect" title="Personal information">personal information</a>".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog20225&#95;1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog20225-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Some researchers include other types of information, for example <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_property" title="Intellectual property">intellectual property</a> or <a href="/wiki/Classified_information" title="Classified information">classified information</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEShukla&#95;et&#95;al.202247–48&#95;5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShukla_et_al.202247–48-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li></ul>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Prevalence">Prevalence</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Data_breach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Prevalence"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1320445320">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}@media print{body.ns-0 .mw-parser-output .hatnote{display:none!important}}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_data_breaches" title="List of data breaches">List of data breaches</a></div>
<p>Before the widespread adoption of <a href="/wiki/Data_breach_notification_laws" title="Data breach notification laws">data breach notification laws</a> around 2005, the prevalence of data breaches is difficult to determine. Even afterwards, annual statistics may be unreliable because breaches are sometimes reported years after they occur,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202218&#95;6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202218-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> or not reported at all.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202229&#95;7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202229-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Nevertheless, the statistics show a continued increase in the number and severity of data breaches that continues as of 2022<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Data_breach&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202217–18&#95;8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202217–18-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 2016, researcher Sasha Romanosky estimated that data breaches (excluding <a href="/wiki/Phishing" title="Phishing">phishing</a>) outnumbered other security breaches by a factor of four.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTENational&#95;Academies&#95;of&#95;Sciences,&#95;Engineering,&#95;and&#95;Medicine20169&#95;9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENational_Academies_of_Sciences,_Engineering,_and_Medicine20169-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Threat_actors">Threat actors</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Data_breach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Threat actors"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Threat_actor" title="Threat actor">Threat actor</a></div>
<p>According to a 2020 estimate, 55 percent of data breaches were caused by <a href="/wiki/Organized_crime" title="Organized crime">organized crime</a>, 10 percent by <a href="/wiki/System_administrators" class="mw-redirect" title="System administrators">system administrators</a>, 10 percent by <a href="/wiki/End_user" title="End user">end users</a> such as customers or employees, and 10 percent by states or state-affiliated actors.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTECrawley202146&#95;10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrawley202146-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Opportunistic criminals may cause data breaches—often using <a href="/wiki/Malware" title="Malware">malware</a> or <a href="/wiki/Social_engineering_attack" class="mw-redirect" title="Social engineering attack">social engineering attacks</a>, but they will typically move on if the security is above average. More organized criminals have more resources and are more focused in their <a href="/wiki/Targeted_threat" title="Targeted threat">targeting of particular data</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler20167–8&#95;11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler20167–8-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Both groups sell the information they obtain for financial gain.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler201613&#95;12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler201613-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Another source of data breaches are <a href="/wiki/Hacktivism" title="Hacktivism">politically motivated hackers</a>, for example <a href="/wiki/Anonymous_(hacker_group)" title="Anonymous (hacker group)">Anonymous</a>, that target particular objectives.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler20169–10&#95;13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler20169–10-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> State-sponsored hackers target either citizens of their country or foreign entities, for such purposes as <a href="/wiki/Political_repression" title="Political repression">political repression</a> and <a href="/wiki/Espionage" title="Espionage">espionage</a>. Often they use undisclosed <a href="/wiki/Zero-day_(computing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Zero-day (computing)">zero-day vulnerabilities</a> for which the hackers are paid large sums of money.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler201610–11&#95;14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler201610–11-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Pegasus_spyware" class="mw-redirect" title="Pegasus spyware">Pegasus spyware</a>—a <a href="/w/index.php?title=No-click_malware&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="No-click malware (page does not exist)">no-click malware</a> developed by the Israeli company <a href="/wiki/NSO_Group" title="NSO Group">NSO Group</a> that can be installed on most cellphones and spies on the users' activity—has drawn attention both for use against criminals such as drug kingpin <a href="/wiki/El_Chapo" class="mw-redirect" title="El Chapo">El Chapo</a> as well as political dissidents, facilitating the <a href="/wiki/Murder_of_Jamal_Khashoggi" class="mw-redirect" title="Murder of Jamal Khashoggi">murder of Jamal Khashoggi</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEKasterEnsign2023355&#95;15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKasterEnsign2023355-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Causes">Causes</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Data_breach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Causes"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Technical_causes">Technical causes</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Data_breach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Technical causes"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Despite developers' goal of delivering a product that works entirely as intended, virtually all <a href="/wiki/Software_bugs" class="mw-redirect" title="Software bugs">software</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hardware_bug" title="Hardware bug">hardware</a> contains bugs.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEAblonBogart20171&#95;16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAblonBogart20171-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> If a bug creates a security risk, it is called a <a href="/wiki/Vulnerability_(computing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Vulnerability (computing)">vulnerability</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEAblonBogart20172&#95;17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAblonBogart20172-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi202125&#95;18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi202125-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESeaman202047–48&#95;19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESeaman202047–48-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Software_patch" class="mw-redirect" title="Software patch">Patches</a> are often released to fix identified vulnerabilities, but those that remain unknown (<a href="/wiki/Zero-day_(computing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Zero-day (computing)">zero days</a>) as well as those that have not been patched are still liable for exploitation.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi202126–27&#95;20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi202126–27-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Both software written by the target of the breach and third party software used by them are vulnerable to attack.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi202125&#95;18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi202125-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Software_vendor_liability" class="mw-redirect" title="Software vendor liability">software vendor is rarely legally liable</a> for the cost of breaches, thus creating an incentive to make cheaper but less secure software.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESloanWarner2019104–105&#95;21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESloanWarner2019104–105-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Vulnerabilities vary in their ability to be <a href="/wiki/Exploit_(computer_security)" title="Exploit (computer security)">exploited</a> by malicious actors. The most valuable allow the attacker to <a href="/wiki/Code_injection" title="Code injection">inject</a> and run their own code (called <a href="/wiki/Malware" title="Malware">malware</a>), without the user being aware of it.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEAblonBogart20172&#95;17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAblonBogart20172-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Some malware is downloaded by users via clicking on a malicious link, but it is also possible for malicious <a href="/wiki/Web_applications" class="mw-redirect" title="Web applications">web applications</a> to download malware just from visiting the website (<a href="/wiki/Drive-by_download" title="Drive-by download">drive-by download</a>). <a href="/wiki/Keyloggers" class="mw-redirect" title="Keyloggers">Keyloggers</a>, a type of malware that records a user's keystrokes, are often used in data breaches.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi202119–22&#95;22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi202119–22-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hash_function" title="Hash function">Hashing</a> is also a good solution for keeping <a href="/wiki/Password" title="Password">passwords</a> safe from <a href="/wiki/Brute-force_attack" title="Brute-force attack">brute-force attacks</a>, but only if the algorithm is sufficiently secure.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTENtantogianMalliarosXenakis2019&#95;23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENtantogianMalliarosXenakis2019-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Many data breaches occur on the hardware operated by a partner of the organization targeted—including the <a href="/wiki/2013_Target_data_breach" class="mw-redirect" title="2013 Target data breach">2013 Target data breach</a> and <a href="/wiki/2014_JPMorgan_Chase_data_breach" title="2014 JPMorgan Chase data breach">2014 JPMorgan Chase data breach</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi202122–23&#95;24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi202122–23-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Outsourcing" title="Outsourcing">Outsourcing</a> work to a third party leads to a risk of data breach if that company has lower security standards; in particular, small companies often lack the resources to take as many security precautions.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler201619–20&#95;25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler201619–20-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi202122–23&#95;24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi202122–23-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> As a result, outsourcing agreements often include security guarantees and provisions for what happens in the event of a data breach.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler201619–20&#95;25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler201619–20-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Human_causes">Human causes</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Data_breach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Human causes"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Human causes of breaches often involve misplaced trust in a malicious actor. <a href="/wiki/Social_engineering_attack" class="mw-redirect" title="Social engineering attack">Social engineering attacks</a> rely on tricking an insider into doing something that compromises the system's security, such as revealing a password or clicking a link to download malware.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESloanWarner201994&#95;26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESloanWarner201994-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Data breaches may also be deliberately caused by insiders.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEMakridis20213&#95;27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMakridis20213-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> One type of social engineering, <a href="/wiki/Phishing" title="Phishing">phishing</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESloanWarner201994&#95;26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESloanWarner201994-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> obtains a user's <a href="/wiki/Credential" title="Credential">credentials</a> by sending them a malicious message impersonating a legitimate entity, such as a bank, and getting the user to enter their credentials onto a malicious website controlled by the cybercriminal. <a href="/wiki/Two-factor_authentication" class="mw-redirect" title="Two-factor authentication">Two-factor authentication</a> can prevent the malicious actor from using the credentials.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi202116–19&#95;28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi202116–19-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Training employees to recognize social engineering is another common strategy.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESloanWarner2019106–107&#95;29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESloanWarner2019106–107-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Another source of breaches is accidental disclosure of information, for example publishing information that should be kept private.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi202128&#95;30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi202128-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler201619&#95;31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler201619-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> With the increase in <a href="/wiki/Remote_work" title="Remote work">remote work</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bring_your_own_device" title="Bring your own device">bring your own device</a> policies, large amounts of corporate data is stored on personal devices of employees. Via carelessness or disregard of company security policies, these devices can be lost or stolen.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler201618–19&#95;32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler201618–19-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Technical solutions can prevent many causes of human error, such as encrypting all sensitive data, preventing employees from using insecure passwords, installing <a href="/wiki/Antivirus_software" title="Antivirus software">antivirus software</a> to prevent malware, and implementing a robust patching system to ensure that all devices are kept up to date.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi202131–32&#95;33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi202131–32-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Breach_lifecycle">Breach lifecycle</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Data_breach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Breach lifecycle"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Prevention">Prevention</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Data_breach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Prevention"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Information_security" title="Information security">Information security</a> and <a href="/wiki/Data_loss_prevention_software" title="Data loss prevention software">Data loss prevention software</a></div>
<p>Although attention to security can reduce the risk of data breach, it cannot bring it to zero. Security is not the only priority of organizations, and an attempt to achieve perfect security would make the technology unusable.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202269–70&#95;34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202269–70-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Many companies hire a <a href="/wiki/Chief_information_security_officer" title="Chief information security officer">chief information security officer</a> (CISO) to oversee the company's information security strategy.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi20217,&#95;9–10&#95;35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi20217,_9–10-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> To obtain information about potential threats, security professionals will network with each other and share information with other organizations facing similar threats.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi2021200–201&#95;36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi2021200–201-36"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>36<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Defense measures can include an updated incident response strategy, contracts with <a href="/wiki/Digital_forensics" title="Digital forensics">digital forensics</a> firms that could investigate a breach,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi2021203–204&#95;37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi2021203–204-37"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cyber_insurance" title="Cyber insurance">cyber insurance</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi2021205&#95;38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi2021205-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler201645&#95;39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler201645-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and monitoring the <a href="/wiki/Dark_web" title="Dark web">dark web</a> for stolen credentials of employees.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi2021206–207&#95;40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi2021206–207-40"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>40<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 2024, the United States' National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) issued a special publication, "Data Confidentiality: Identifying and Protecting Assets Against Data Breaches".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFisher&#95;&#39;&#39;et&#95;al.&#39;&#39;2024Title&#95;page&#95;41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFisher_&#39;&#39;et_al.&#39;&#39;2024Title_page-41"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/NIST_Cybersecurity_Framework" title="NIST Cybersecurity Framework">NIST Cybersecurity Framework</a> also contains information about data protection.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFisher&#95;&#39;&#39;et&#95;al.&#39;&#39;20242&#95;42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFisher_&#39;&#39;et_al.&#39;&#39;20242-42"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>42<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Other organizations have released different standards for data protection.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler2016210&#95;43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler2016210-43"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>43<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The architecture of a company's systems plays a key role in deterring attackers. Daswani and Elbayadi recommend having only one means of <a href="/wiki/Authentication" title="Authentication">authentication</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi2021217&#95;44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi2021217-44"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>44<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> avoiding redundant systems, and making the most secure setting default.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi2021215–216&#95;45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi2021215–216-45"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>45<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Defense_in_depth_(computing)" title="Defense in depth (computing)">Defense in depth</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Distributed_privilege&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Distributed privilege (page does not exist)">distributed privilege</a>—requiring multiple authentications to perform an operation—can make systems more difficult to compromise.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTETjoa&#95;&#39;&#39;et&#95;al.&#39;&#39;202414&#95;46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETjoa_&#39;&#39;et_al.&#39;&#39;202414-46"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>46<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Giving employees and software the least amount of access necessary to fulfill their functions (<a href="/wiki/Principle_of_least_privilege" title="Principle of least privilege">principle of least privilege</a>) limits the likelihood and damage of breaches.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi2021217&#95;44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi2021217-44"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>44<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTELenhard202253&#95;47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELenhard202253-47"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>47<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Several data breaches were enabled by reliance on <a href="/wiki/Security_by_obscurity" class="mw-redirect" title="Security by obscurity">security by obscurity</a>; the victims had put access credentials in publicly accessible files.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi2021218&#95;48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi2021218-48"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>48<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Nevertheless, prioritizing ease of use is also important because otherwise users might circumvent the security systems.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi2021218–219&#95;49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi2021218–219-49"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>49<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Rigorous <a href="/wiki/Software_testing" title="Software testing">software testing</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Penetration_testing" class="mw-redirect" title="Penetration testing">penetration testing</a>, can reduce software vulnerabilities, and must be performed prior to each release even if the company is using a <a href="/wiki/CI/CD" title="CI/CD">continuous integration/continuous deployment</a> model where new versions are constantly being rolled out.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi2021314–315&#95;50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDaswaniElbayadi2021314–315-50"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>50<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The principle of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Least_persistence&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Least persistence (page does not exist)">least persistence</a><sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTETjoa&#95;&#39;&#39;et&#95;al.&#39;&#39;202468&#95;51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETjoa_&#39;&#39;et_al.&#39;&#39;202468-51"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>51<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>—avoiding the collection of data that is not necessary and destruction of data that is no longer necessary—can mitigate the harm from breaches.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTELenhard202260&#95;52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELenhard202260-52"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>52<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler2016184&#95;53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler2016184-53"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>53<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog2022146&#95;54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog2022146-54"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>54<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The challenge is that destroying data can be more complex with modern database systems.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTETjoa&#95;&#39;&#39;et&#95;al.&#39;&#39;202469&#95;55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETjoa_&#39;&#39;et_al.&#39;&#39;202469-55"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>55<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Response">Response</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Data_breach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Response"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Computer_emergency_response_team" title="Computer emergency response team">Computer security incident response team</a></div>
<p>Many data breaches are never detected.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTECrawley202139&#95;56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrawley202139-56"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>56<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Of those that are, most breaches are detected by third parties;<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler201664&#95;57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler201664-57"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>57<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTENational&#95;Academies&#95;of&#95;Sciences,&#95;Engineering,&#95;and&#95;Medicine201625&#95;58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENational_Academies_of_Sciences,_Engineering,_and_Medicine201625-58"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>58<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> others are detected by employees or automated systems.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler20164&#95;59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler20164-59"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>59<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Responding to breaches is often the responsibility of a dedicated <a href="/wiki/Computer_emergency_response_team" title="Computer emergency response team">computer security incident response team</a>, often including technical experts, <a href="/wiki/Public_relations" title="Public relations">public relations</a>, and legal counsel.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTECrawley202197&#95;60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrawley202197-60"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>60<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler20165,&#95;32&#95;61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler20165,_32-61"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>61<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Many companies do not have sufficient expertise in-house, and subcontract some of these roles;<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler201686&#95;62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler201686-62"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>62<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> often, these outside resources are provided by the cyber insurance policy.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler201694&#95;63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler201694-63"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>63<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> After a data breach becomes known to the company, the next steps typically include confirming it occurred, notifying the response team, and attempting to contain the damage.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler20164–5&#95;64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler20164–5-64"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>64<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>To stop exfiltration of data, common strategies include shutting down affected servers, taking them offline, <a href="/wiki/Software_patch" class="mw-redirect" title="Software patch">patching</a> the vulnerability, and <a href="/wiki/Software_build" title="Software build">rebuilding</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler2016120–122&#95;65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler2016120–122-65"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>65<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Once the exact way that the data was compromised is identified, there is typically only one or two technical vulnerabilities that need to be addressed in order to contain the breach and prevent it from reoccurring.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler2016115&#95;66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler2016115-66"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>66<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Penetration_test" title="Penetration test">penetration test</a> can then verify that the fix is working as expected.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler2016116&#95;67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler2016116-67"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>67<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> If <a href="/wiki/Malware" title="Malware">malware</a> is involved, the organization must investigate and close all infiltration and exfiltration vectors, as well as locate and remove all malware from its systems.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler2016117–118&#95;68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler2016117–118-68"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>68<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> If data was posted on the <a href="/wiki/Dark_web" title="Dark web">dark web</a>, companies may attempt to have it taken down.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler2016119&#95;69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler2016119-69"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>69<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Containing the breach can compromise investigation, and some tactics (such as shutting down servers) can violate the company's contractual obligations.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler2016124&#95;70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler2016124-70"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>70<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Gathering data about the breach can facilitate later litigation or criminal prosecution,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler201681–82&#95;71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler201681–82-71"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>71<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> but only if the data is gathered according to legal standards and the <a href="/wiki/Chain_of_custody" title="Chain of custody">chain of custody</a> is maintained.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler201683&#95;72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler201683-72"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>72<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Database forensics can narrow down the records involved, limiting the scope of the incident.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler2016128&#95;73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler2016128-73"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>73<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Extensive investigation may be undertaken, which can be even more expensive than <a href="/wiki/Litigation" class="mw-redirect" title="Litigation">litigation</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTENational&#95;Academies&#95;of&#95;Sciences,&#95;Engineering,&#95;and&#95;Medicine201625&#95;58-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENational_Academies_of_Sciences,_Engineering,_and_Medicine201625-58"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>58<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In the United States, breaches may be investigated by government agencies such as the <a href="/wiki/Office_for_Civil_Rights" title="Office for Civil Rights">Office for Civil Rights</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Health_and_Human_Services" title="United States Department of Health and Human Services">United States Department of Health and Human Services</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Trade_Commission" title="Federal Trade Commission">Federal Trade Commission</a> (FTC).<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTENational&#95;Academies&#95;of&#95;Sciences,&#95;Engineering,&#95;and&#95;Medicine201622&#95;74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENational_Academies_of_Sciences,_Engineering,_and_Medicine201622-74"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>74<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Law enforcement agencies may investigate breaches<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler201644&#95;75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler201644-75"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>75<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> although the hackers responsible are rarely caught.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202258&#95;76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202258-76"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>76<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Notifications are typically sent out as required by law.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler20165,&#95;44&#95;77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler20165,_44-77"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>77<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Many companies offer free <a href="/wiki/Credit_monitoring" class="mw-redirect" title="Credit monitoring">credit monitoring</a> to people affected by a data breach, although only around 5 percent of those eligible take advantage of the service.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTENational&#95;Academies&#95;of&#95;Sciences,&#95;Engineering,&#95;and&#95;Medicine201613&#95;78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENational_Academies_of_Sciences,_Engineering,_and_Medicine201613-78"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>78<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Issuing new credit cards to consumers, although expensive, is an effective strategy to reduce the risk of <a href="/wiki/Credit_card_fraud" title="Credit card fraud">credit card fraud</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTENational&#95;Academies&#95;of&#95;Sciences,&#95;Engineering,&#95;and&#95;Medicine201613&#95;78-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENational_Academies_of_Sciences,_Engineering,_and_Medicine201613-78"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>78<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Companies try to restore trust in their business operations and take steps to prevent a breach from reoccurring.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler20165–6&#95;79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler20165–6-79"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>79<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Consequences">Consequences</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Data_breach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Consequences"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="For_consumers">For consumers</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Data_breach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: For consumers"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>After a data breach, criminals make money by selling data, such as usernames, passwords, <a href="/wiki/Social_media" title="Social media">social media</a> or <a href="/wiki/Loyalty_business_model" title="Loyalty business model">customer loyalty</a> account information, <a href="/wiki/Debit_card" title="Debit card">debit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Credit_card" title="Credit card">credit card</a> numbers,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler201613&#95;12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler201613-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and personal health information (see <a href="/wiki/Medical_data_breach" title="Medical data breach">medical data breach</a>).<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler201614&#95;80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler201614-80"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>80<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Criminals often sell this data on the <a href="/wiki/Dark_web" title="Dark web">dark web</a>—parts of the internet where it is difficult to trace users and illicit activity is widespread—using platforms like <a href="/wiki/.onion" title=".onion">.onion</a> or <a href="/wiki/I2P" title="I2P">I2P</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler201612–13&#95;81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler201612–13-81"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>81<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Originating in the 2000s, the dark web, followed by untraceable <a href="/wiki/Cryptocurrencies" class="mw-redirect" title="Cryptocurrencies">cryptocurrencies</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Bitcoin" title="Bitcoin">Bitcoin</a> in the 2010s, made it possible for criminals to sell data obtained in breaches with minimal risk of getting caught, facilitating an increase in hacking.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEDavidoff2019&quot;Modern&#95;dark&#95;data&#95;brokers&quot;&#95;82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavidoff2019&quot;Modern_dark_data_brokers&quot;-82"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>82<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202221&#95;83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202221-83"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>83<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> One popular darknet marketplace, <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road_(marketplace)" title="Silk Road (marketplace)">Silk Road</a>, was shut down in 2013 and its operators arrested, but several other marketplaces emerged in its place.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>84<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Telegram_(software)" title="Telegram (software)">Telegram</a> is also a popular forum for illegal sales of data.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>85<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>This information may be used for a variety of purposes, such as <a href="/wiki/Spamming" title="Spamming">spamming</a>, obtaining products with a victim's loyalty or payment information, <a href="/wiki/Identity_theft" title="Identity theft">identity theft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drug_fraud" title="Drug fraud">prescription drug fraud</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Insurance_fraud" title="Insurance fraud">insurance fraud</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler201613–14&#95;86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler201613–14-86"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>86<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The threat of data breach or revealing information obtained in a data breach can be used for <a href="/wiki/Extortion" title="Extortion">extortion</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler201613&#95;12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler201613-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Consumers may suffer various forms of tangible or intangible harm from the theft of their personal data, or not notice any harm.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTENational&#95;Academies&#95;of&#95;Sciences,&#95;Engineering,&#95;and&#95;Medicine201627&#95;87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENational_Academies_of_Sciences,_Engineering,_and_Medicine201627-87"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>87<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A significant portion of those affected by a data breach become victims of <a href="/wiki/Identity_theft" title="Identity theft">identity theft</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTENational&#95;Academies&#95;of&#95;Sciences,&#95;Engineering,&#95;and&#95;Medicine201613&#95;78-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENational_Academies_of_Sciences,_Engineering,_and_Medicine201613-78"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>78<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A person's identifying information often circulates on the dark web for years, causing an increased risk of identity theft regardless of remediation efforts.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202258&#95;76-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202258-76"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>76<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTENational&#95;Academies&#95;of&#95;Sciences,&#95;Engineering,&#95;and&#95;Medicine201630–31&#95;88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENational_Academies_of_Sciences,_Engineering,_and_Medicine201630–31-88"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>88<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Even if a customer does not end up footing the bill for <a href="/wiki/Credit_card_fraud" title="Credit card fraud">credit card fraud</a> or identity theft, they have to spend time resolving the situation.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTENational&#95;Academies&#95;of&#95;Sciences,&#95;Engineering,&#95;and&#95;Medicine201629&#95;89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENational_Academies_of_Sciences,_Engineering,_and_Medicine201629-89"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>89<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202256&#95;90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202256-90"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>90<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Intangible harms include <a href="/wiki/Doxxing" class="mw-redirect" title="Doxxing">doxxing</a> (publicly revealing someone's personal information), for example medication usage or personal photos.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTENational&#95;Academies&#95;of&#95;Sciences,&#95;Engineering,&#95;and&#95;Medicine201627–29&#95;91-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENational_Academies_of_Sciences,_Engineering,_and_Medicine201627–29-91"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>91<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="For_organizations">For organizations</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Data_breach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: For organizations"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Impacts on the company can range from lost business, reduced employee productivity due to systems being offline or personnel redirected to working on the breach,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler201622&#95;92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler201622-92"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>92<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> resignation or firing of senior executives,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTENational&#95;Academies&#95;of&#95;Sciences,&#95;Engineering,&#95;and&#95;Medicine201622&#95;74-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENational_Academies_of_Sciences,_Engineering,_and_Medicine201622-74"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>74<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Reputational_damage" title="Reputational damage">reputational damage</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTENational&#95;Academies&#95;of&#95;Sciences,&#95;Engineering,&#95;and&#95;Medicine201622&#95;74-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENational_Academies_of_Sciences,_Engineering,_and_Medicine201622-74"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>74<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler201641&#95;93-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler201641-93"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>93<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and increasing the future cost of auditing or security.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTENational&#95;Academies&#95;of&#95;Sciences,&#95;Engineering,&#95;and&#95;Medicine201622&#95;74-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENational_Academies_of_Sciences,_Engineering,_and_Medicine201622-74"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>74<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> There is some evidence suggesting a temporary, short-term decline in <a href="/wiki/Share_price" title="Share price">stock price</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEMakridis20211&#95;94-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMakridis20211-94"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>94<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>   Consumer losses from a breach are usually a negative <a href="/wiki/Externality" title="Externality">externality</a> for the business.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESloanWarner2019104&#95;95-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESloanWarner2019104-95"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>95<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Some experts have argued that the evidence suggests there is not enough direct costs or reputational damage from data breaches to sufficiently <a href="/wiki/Incentive" title="Incentive">incentivize</a> their prevention.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEMakridis20211,&#95;7&#95;96-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMakridis20211,_7-96"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>96<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESloanWarner201964&#95;97-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESloanWarner201964-97"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>97<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Estimating the cost of data breaches is difficult, both because not all breaches are reported and also because calculating the impact of breaches in financial terms is not straightforward. There are multiple ways of calculating the cost to businesses, especially when it comes to personnel time dedicated to dealing with the breach.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTENational&#95;Academies&#95;of&#95;Sciences,&#95;Engineering,&#95;and&#95;Medicine20168–10&#95;98-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENational_Academies_of_Sciences,_Engineering,_and_Medicine20168–10-98"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>98<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Author Kevvie Fowler estimates that more than half the direct cost incurred by companies is in the form of litigation expenses and services provided to affected individuals, with the remaining cost split between notification and detection, including forensics and investigation. He argues that these costs are reduced if the organization has invested in security prior to the breach or has previous experience with breaches. The more <a href="/wiki/Record_(computer_science)" title="Record (computer science)">data records</a> involved, the more expensive a breach typically will be.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler201621&#95;99-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler201621-99"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>99<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 2016, researcher <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sasha_Romanosky&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sasha Romanosky (page does not exist)">Sasha Romanosky</a> estimated that while the mean breach cost around the targeted firm $5 million, this figure was inflated by a few highly expensive breaches, and the typical data breach was much less costly, around $200,000. Romanosky estimated the total annual cost to corporations in the United States to be around $10 billion.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTENational&#95;Academies&#95;of&#95;Sciences,&#95;Engineering,&#95;and&#95;Medicine201610&#95;100-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENational_Academies_of_Sciences,_Engineering,_and_Medicine201610-100"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>100<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It was estimated a 2025 data breach at cryptocurrency exchange <a href="/wiki/Coinbase" title="Coinbase">Coinbase</a> lead to as much as $400 million in total loss including damage to customers, a ransom demanded by hackers, legal costs, lost crypto assets, reputational fallout, and compliance.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>101<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>102<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Laws">Laws</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Data_breach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Laws"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Notification">Notification</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Data_breach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Notification"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Data_breach_notification_laws" title="Data breach notification laws">Data breach notification laws</a></div>
<p>The law regarding data breaches is often found in <a href="/wiki/Privacy_law" title="Privacy law">legislation to protect privacy</a> more generally, and is dominated by provisions mandating notification when breaches occur.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202210&#95;103-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202210-103"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>103<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Laws differ in how breaches are defined,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202241&#95;104-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202241-104"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>104<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> what type of information is protected, the deadline for notification,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202242&#95;105-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202242-105"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>105<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and who has <a href="/wiki/Standing_(law)" title="Standing (law)">standing</a> to sue if the law is violated.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202243&#95;106-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202243-106"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>106<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Notification laws increase <a href="/wiki/Transparency_(behavior)" title="Transparency (behavior)">transparency</a> and provide a reputational incentive for companies to reduce breaches.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202244&#95;107-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202244-107"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>107<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The cost of notifying the breach can be high if many people were affected and is incurred regardless of the company's responsibility, so it can function like a <a href="/wiki/Strict_liability" title="Strict liability">strict liability</a> fine.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202245&#95;108-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202245-108"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>108<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>As of 2024<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Data_breach&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, <i>Thomas on Data Breach</i> listed 62 <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_member_states" class="mw-redirect" title="United Nations member states">United Nations member states</a> that are covered by data breach notification laws. Some other countries require breach notification in more general <a href="/wiki/Information_privacy_law" title="Information privacy law">data protection laws</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEThomas2023xxvii,&#95;xxix,&#95;xxxii–xxxiii,&#95;xxxiv&#95;109-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomas2023xxvii,_xxix,_xxxii–xxxiii,_xxxiv-109"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>109<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Shortly after the first reported data breach in April 2002, California passed <a href="/wiki/California_Senate_Bill_1386_(2002)" title="California Senate Bill 1386 (2002)">a law requiring notification</a> when an individual's personal information was breached.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTELesemann2010206&#95;110-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELesemann2010206-110"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>110<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In the United States, notification laws proliferated after the February 2005 <a href="/w/index.php?title=ChoicePoint_data_breach&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="ChoicePoint data breach (page does not exist)">ChoicePoint data breach</a>, widely publicized in part because of the large number of people affected (more than 140,000) and also because of outrage that the company initially informed only affected people in California.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTELesemann2010206–207&#95;111-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELesemann2010206–207-111"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>111<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEJoerling2010468–469&#95;112-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoerling2010468–469-112"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>112<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 2018, the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>'s <a href="/wiki/General_Data_Protection_Regulation" title="General Data Protection Regulation">General Data Protection Regulation</a> (GDPR) took effect. The GDPR requires notification within 72 hours, with very high fines possible for large companies not in compliance. This regulation also stimulated the tightening of data privacy laws elsewhere.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESeaman20206–7&#95;113-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESeaman20206–7-113"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>113<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202240&#95;114-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202240-114"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>114<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> As of 2022<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Data_breach&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, the only <a href="/wiki/United_States_federal_law" class="mw-redirect" title="United States federal law">United States federal law</a> requiring notification for data breaches is limited to medical data regulated under <a href="/wiki/HIPAA" class="mw-redirect" title="HIPAA">HIPAA</a>, but all 50 states (since Alabama passed a law in 2018) have their own general data breach notification laws.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202240&#95;114-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202240-114"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>114<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Security_safeguards">Security safeguards</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Data_breach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Security safeguards"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Measures to protect data from a breach are typically absent from the law or vague.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202210&#95;103-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202210-103"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>103<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Filling this gap is standards required by <a href="/wiki/Cyber_insurance" title="Cyber insurance">cyber insurance</a>, which is held by most large companies and <a href="/wiki/Insurance_as_regulation" title="Insurance as regulation">functions as <i>de facto</i> regulation</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTENational&#95;Academies&#95;of&#95;Sciences,&#95;Engineering,&#95;and&#95;Medicine201624&#95;115-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENational_Academies_of_Sciences,_Engineering,_and_Medicine201624-115"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>115<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTETalesh2018237&#95;116-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETalesh2018237-116"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>116<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Of the laws that do exist, there are two main approaches—one that prescribes specific standards to follow, and the <a href="/wiki/Reasonableness" title="Reasonableness">reasonableness</a> approach.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202248&#95;117-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202248-117"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>117<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The former is rarely used due to a lack of flexibility and reluctance of legislators to arbitrate technical issues; with the latter approach, the law is vague but specific standards can emerge from <a href="/wiki/Case_law" title="Case law">case law</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202248–49&#95;118-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202248–49-118"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>118<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Companies often prefer the standards approach for providing greater <a href="/wiki/Legal_certainty" title="Legal certainty">legal certainty</a>, but they might check all the boxes without providing a secure product.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202252&#95;119-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202252-119"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>119<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> An additional flaw is that the laws are poorly enforced, with penalties often much less than the cost of a breach, and many companies do not follow them.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202253&#95;120-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202253-120"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>120<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Litigation">Litigation</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Data_breach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Litigation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Class-action_lawsuit" class="mw-redirect" title="Class-action lawsuit">class-action lawsuits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Derivative_suit" title="Derivative suit">derivative suits</a>, and other litigation have been brought after data breaches.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler20165&#95;121-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler20165-121"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>121<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> They are often <a href="/wiki/Settlement_(litigation)" title="Settlement (litigation)">settled</a> regardless of the merits of the case due to the high cost of litigation.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTEFowler2016222&#95;122-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler2016222-122"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>122<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202255,&#95;59&#95;123-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202255,_59-123"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>123<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Even if a settlement is paid, few affected consumers receive any money as it usually is only cents to a few dollars per victim.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTENational&#95;Academies&#95;of&#95;Sciences,&#95;Engineering,&#95;and&#95;Medicine201622&#95;74-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENational_Academies_of_Sciences,_Engineering,_and_Medicine201622-74"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>74<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202255,&#95;59&#95;123-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202255,_59-123"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>123<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Legal scholars <a href="/wiki/Daniel_J._Solove" title="Daniel J. Solove">Daniel J. Solove</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Woodrow_Hartzog&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Woodrow Hartzog (page does not exist)">Woodrow Hartzog</a> argue that "Litigation has increased the costs of data breaches but has accomplished little else."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202255&#95;124-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202255-124"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>124<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Plaintiffs often struggle to prove that they suffered harm from a data breach.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202255&#95;124-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202255-124"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>124<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The contribution of a company's actions to a data breach varies,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202253&#95;120-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESoloveHartzog202253-120"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>120<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTENational&#95;Academies&#95;of&#95;Sciences,&#95;Engineering,&#95;and&#95;Medicine201623&#95;125-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENational_Academies_of_Sciences,_Engineering,_and_Medicine201623-125"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>125<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and likewise the liability for the damage resulting for data breaches is a contested matter. It is disputed what standard should be applied, whether it is strict liability, <a href="/wiki/Negligence" title="Negligence">negligence</a>, or something else.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTENational&#95;Academies&#95;of&#95;Sciences,&#95;Engineering,&#95;and&#95;Medicine201623&#95;125-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENational_Academies_of_Sciences,_Engineering,_and_Medicine201623-125"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>125<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
